# Care Economy Funding — Design Draft

**Status:** Proposed — design draft, not incorporated. The constitution is unchanged.

**Problem (capstone completeness gap):** Unpaid care — childrearing, eldercare, mutual aid, disability support — is *recognized* in the corpus but never *funded*. Annex K §K3 admits care as a contribution class for Voice and Service Record, and Humane Constitution §432 names "the patient work of raising children and caring for the elderly" among the most important contributions a person makes. But recognition stops at civic eligibility. Care earns standing; it never earns survival. Since care labor falls disproportionately on women, the elderly, and the poor, a system that credits care toward civic role-eligibility but routes all *material* support through paid Flow work silently recreates the gendered class hierarchy the corpus elsewhere warns against (Annex K §K3 "anti-legibility trap"). This is an in-scope improvement to the distributive instruments selected in the [wealth instrument panel decision](2026-06-03-wealth-instrument-panel-decision.md).

---

## The core tension (name it plainly)

To fund care is to risk one of two opposite failures. **Underpay it** and you reaffirm that love-work is worthless because it is unpriced — the very injustice. **Price it** and you commodify intimacy: a parent comforting a child, a daughter sitting with a dying mother, becomes a billable unit, and the home becomes a worksite the state must measure. The corpus calls reducing persons to data/compliance a dignity failure, and treating the unpriceable as priceable a Babel temptation — the pride that everything of worth can be counted and controlled. Care must be *valued* without being *transacted*. The design below resolves this by funding the **conditions and the carer**, never the *act* of caring, and by verifying *life-circumstance* (a status held), not *performance* (a service rendered).

---

## Three options weighed

**Option 1 — Care-weight the endowment / dividend.** Supplement the citizen's dividend for people in documented care years, or grant extra universal capital endowment. *Strength:* rides distribution rails the wealth design already builds (social wealth fund → dividend, into Flow above the Essential Access wall); no new instrument, no new wall risk. *Weakness:* a dividend top-up alone treats care as a finance problem and can read as a small bribe rather than recognition; the endowment is one-time and age-gated, so it misses mid-life carers.

**Option 2 — Direct care payment / credit (the "wages for housework" lever).** Pay carers a Flow allowance for hours of care. *Strength:* unambiguously material; closest to wage parity. *Weakness:* maximal on every failure mode. It commodifies intimacy by definition (it *is* a wage for love). To resist fraud it demands hour-logging and outcome checks — i.e. **home surveillance**, which fails gate G-3 (no panopticon) outright, exactly as confiscatory net-worth demurrage did in the wealth decision. And a "care wage" tied to a paid-work logic drifts toward a *buyable* status, pressing on the walls.

**Option 3 — Care recognized in the floor.** Guarantee carers an adequate Essential Access floor plus Protected-Pause-style civic continuity (Annex K §K4) so caregiving never costs survival or civic standing. *Strength:* lowest dignity and surveillance cost — Essential Access is unbuyable, non-transferable, and verified by *need*, not performance (Humane Constitution §350–355); it cannot reduce care to a transaction because it is not a payment for the care at all. *Weakness:* a floor alone is *only* a floor — it secures survival but does not share in society's *wealth*, so it can leave carers permanently at subsistence while paid peers accumulate, which is the static-advantage loop in slow motion.

---

## Recommended design: a blend, floor-first

Adopt **Option 3 as the spine** (it is the one that clears every gate cleanly), with a **bounded Option-1 supplement** layered on the existing distribution rails, and **Option 2 explicitly rejected** as the model for the recognition itself.

### Layer A — Care never costs survival or standing (floor + continuity)

1. **Care-Active status** is a recognized life-circumstance under Annex K §K4's existing Protected Pause family — not a job. A person in a documented care year holds Care-Active status the way a person in crisis holds a Protected Pause.
2. While Care-Active: the person's **Essential Access floor is guaranteed in full** and the Care-Active period **does not erode Voice or Service Record** (extends §K4's civic-floor and asymmetric-decay protections; aligns with §580 participation continuity). Caregiving therefore cannot sever survival or civic life — the §K3 hierarchy is broken at its root.
3. This needs **no new wall and no convertibility**: Essential Access stays unbuyable and non-transferable (§214); Care-Active is a *status that protects access*, never a credit that purchases it.

### Layer B — Care shares in shared wealth (bounded dividend supplement)

4. A **Carer's Dividend Supplement** rides the **citizen's dividend** channel already selected in the wealth decision (passive social wealth fund → dividend → Flow, above the Essential Access wall). It is a **flat, per-care-year uplift** — *not* per hour, *not* per child, *not* outcome-scaled.
5. Flat-per-period is the dignity firewall: because the supplement does not vary with *how much* or *how well* you care, there is **nothing to surveil and nothing to bill**. You are not paid for the act of love; you receive a share of common wealth *while* you do society's unpaid work, the way the dividend already shares land rent with everyone. This is care *valued*, not care *transacted*.
6. Capped and non-accumulative across the wall: the supplement enters Flow and behaves like any dividend; it never touches Voice (which stays non-accumulative, §219) or Service Record (which stays non-tradable, §577).

### Layer C — Lifetime equity (optional endowment top-up)

7. For sustained care (e.g. multi-year primary caregiving), a **one-time capital-endowment top-up** counters the lifetime wealth gap Layer B's flow cannot close — answering Option 3's weakness directly. Pilot-gated and modest; flagged as the most contestable element and the first to drop if it strains the social wealth fund.

## Verification without a panopticon (gate G-3)

The verification model is **attribute attestation at the registry layer**, mirroring how the corpus already handles need-tiers and contribution (Annex K §K3 multi-path verification, §K6 data minimization) — **never observation of the home**.

- **Self-declaration is the baseline.** Per §K3's no-attester fallback, a person declares Care-Active status; the claim is not denied for lack of witnesses, and isolation never disqualifies. Light corroboration (an existing dependent record, an eldercare relationship already on file, a community attestation) may *raise* a claim but is never required to *open* one.
- **Verify status, not performance.** The registry confirms a *relationship and a period* ("this person is the primary carer of a dependent this year"), not *what was done in the home*. No hour logs, no visits, no outcome audits — those are the Option-2 surveillance machinery the design refuses.
- **Burden scales with stake, not need (§K3).** The flat supplement and floor protection are low-value, so they carry the lightest verification. Only the Layer-C endowment top-up — a larger, durable benefit — invites heavier (but still home-free) corroboration.
- **Data minimization (§K6) binds the whole record.** Care-Active and attestation data is collected only for the floor/continuity/supplement purpose, retention-limited, access-logged, and **never repurposed** for enforcement, profiling, or targeting. The recognition apparatus must not become a surveillance archive of family life.
- **Presumption of good faith (§K4).** As with Protected Pause, review begins from good faith and demands only minimal evidence; relief is never conditioned on exhaustive proof or re-telling.

## Evaluation gates

- **G-3 (no panopticon):** Cleared. No home observation; verification is registry-layer attribute attestation of a held status, with self-declaration baseline. Option 2 (hour-logging) was rejected precisely here.
- **Dignity:** Cleared. Flat, performance-blind support funds the carer and the conditions, never the act; care is valued without being priced or measured.
- **Distributive-justice family 3 (care & non-market work):** This *is* the family — the design moves care from civic-credit-only to materially supported, attacking the gendered static-advantage loop.
- **Walls:** Intact. Essential Access stays unbuyable/non-transferable; the supplement lives in Flow via the existing dividend channel; Voice and Service Record are untouched and non-convertible. Care-Active is a protective status, never a buyable rung.

## Christ-centered review

**Alignment.** Jesus measured the widow's two copper coins as greater than every large gift (Mark 12:41–44) — worth is not the priced magnitude but the love behind the unseen act. He identified care for the hungry, sick, and imprisoned as service rendered to Him directly (Matt 25:35–40), the highest service, almost all of it unpaid. A system that credits only what the market prices implicitly calls the uncredentialed carer worthless; this design refuses that, securing the carer's bread and civic voice and granting a share of common wealth, while deliberately *not* pricing the love itself.

**Dignity.** Funding *status and conditions* rather than logging *performance* keeps the carer a person, not a metered service-unit, and keeps the home a home, not a worksite under inspection — honoring the person over the system (Mark 10:42–45).

**Fruit (Matt 7:16–20).** The good fruit sought: a parent or daughter can give years to care without falling into poverty or losing civic standing, and without the state watching the bedside. The Babel risk guarded against — the pride that all worth can be counted and controlled — is held off precisely by the flat, unmeasured form of the support: the system funds care without presuming to price it, and remains, like the rest of the corpus, a human framework that serves people under God rather than a measure of their worth before Him.
