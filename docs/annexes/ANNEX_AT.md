## AT0 — Problem Diagnosed

The Humane Constitution's transition outline (Phase 2) covers external trade in two sentences: essential-capacity exports are capped to protect domestic Essential Access guarantees, and foreign capital converts to Flow subject to demurrage. This is structurally correct but architecturally incomplete. The gap creates four specific failure modes:

**AT0.1 — Dependency capture.** If domestic production of any Essential Access category falls below survival-critical levels and the deficit is filled by a single foreign supplier or supply chain, that supplier acquires effective coercive leverage over the protocol's survival floor. The non-convertibility firewall works internally; it provides no protection against an external party who controls physical supply.

**AT0.2 — Export-induced domestic shortage.** Without explicit export caps, market prices for domestically produced essentials will draw inventory outward during global scarcity events, precisely when domestic buffers need to be largest. The Article III reserve architecture buffers this but cannot absorb an indefinite export drain.

**AT0.3 — Capital-inflow arbitrage.** External capital denominated in legacy currency enters the Flow system as productive investment. Without a mandatory conversion and demurrage treatment, external capital can effectively create hoarding-resistant purchasing power by cycling in and out — importing accumulation dynamics the internal architecture was designed to prevent.

**AT0.4 — Reciprocity void.** The protocol has no defined response to a counterparty that practices extraction: suppressing wages in traded goods sectors to undercut domestic production, blocking protocol-compliant imports in retaliation for non-monetary governance structures, or weaponizing supply-chain dependencies during political disputes. Without a defined reciprocity mechanism, the protocol's open posture becomes a one-way vulnerability.

This annex establishes the architecture that closes all four failure modes. It does not establish a protectionist or autarkic posture — the protocol trades freely for abundance. It establishes the floor below which dependence is not permitted, the cap above which export is not permitted during stress, the conversion rules for incoming capital, and the reciprocity mechanism that adjusts trade posture in response to counterparty behavior.

## AT1 — Strategic Floors

### AT1.1 — Definition

A **strategic floor** is a minimum domestic production and reserve capacity maintained for each Essential Access category regardless of trade economics. The strategic floor is not the total domestic production target — it is the irreducible minimum below which the protocol will not allow domestic capacity to fall even when imports are cheaper, more abundant, or otherwise economically superior.

The strategic floor exists because survival access must never depend on a counterparty's goodwill, political stability, or continued participation in trade. Trade is the mechanism for abundance above the floor; the floor is the mechanism for survival when trade fails.

### AT1.2 — Floor Categories

Strategic floors apply to each category in the Essential Access basket plus critical infrastructure dependencies:

| Category | Floor Definition | Governing Annex |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Caloric staples** | Domestic production + committed strategic reserves ≥ 180 days of verified population demand at CSM caloric baseline | Annex Y §Y1 |
| **Potable water** | Domestic sourcing capacity ≥ 100% of CSM water baseline under worst-case drought scenario per Article III measurement | Annex M |
| **Energy (heating, cooking, essential power)** | Domestic generation + contracted regional supply ≥ 90 days of CSM energy baseline during peak-demand period | Annex Y §Y1 |
| **Essential medicines (CSM-designated)** | Domestic manufacturing or committed strategic stockpile ≥ 365 days of forecast demand for each CSM-designated medicine | Annex Y §Y1 |
| **Basic shelter materials** | Domestic production capacity sufficient to meet emergency replacement needs for 5% of housing stock per year without import dependency | Article V |
| **Communications infrastructure** | Core protocol network must operate without dependency on foreign-owned infrastructure for essential functions | Article VII |
| **Seed stock and agricultural inputs** | Domestic or treaty-secured supply sufficient for two consecutive planting seasons independent of spot markets | Annex M |

### AT1.3 — Floor Verification

Strategic floors are verified by the Article III oracle network as a designated measurement category. Floor verification is published quarterly alongside the standard capacity indices. A floor breach — any category falling below its strategic floor definition — triggers:

1. Immediate notification to Article VI deliberative bodies and Article VII transparency dashboards
2. Automatic activation of domestic production incentive channels (Article V PFCR) to close the gap
3. Suspension of export permissions for the breached category (§AT3) pending floor restoration
4. Escalation to the Federated Ombuds (Annex AI) if the breach persists beyond 90 days

### AT1.4 — Floor Amendment

Strategic floor definitions are Tier 2 operational parameters except where they directly govern a CSM basket category, in which case they are Tier 1 adjacent. Reduction of any floor touching a CSM category requires H-2 supermajority and 90-day deliberation. Increase of any floor requires standard FAP. The oracle network may not adjust floor definitions through measurement methodology changes without explicit amendment authorization.

## AT2 — Import Dependency Thresholds

### AT2.1 — Dependency Classification

Import dependence on a single supplier or supply chain creates capture risk even when strategic floors are technically met. This section establishes concentration limits that operate independently of floor calculations.

| Dependency Level | Definition | Protocol Response |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Safe** | No single foreign source supplies > 25% of any Essential Access category's domestic consumption | No action required; routine monitoring |
| **Watch** | A single foreign source supplies 26–40% of any Essential Access category | Publish concentration report; activate domestic production incentive; begin diversification planning |
| **Elevated** | A single foreign source supplies 41–60% of any Essential Access category | Mandatory diversification plan with published milestones; PFCR-funded domestic capacity investment activated; export permit review triggered |
| **Critical** | A single foreign source supplies > 60% of any Essential Access category | Treat as equivalent to a floor breach; emergency diversification authority; potential temporary import controls on the concentrated category pending diversification |

### AT2.2 — Measurement

Dependency percentages are measured on a rolling 12-month basis and include indirect dependencies — a domestic supplier that sources > 60% of a key input from a single foreign source inherits the concentration risk of that input for classification purposes. The oracle network publishes supply-chain concentration maps for Essential Access categories as a standard quarterly deliverable.

### AT2.3 — Concentration Risk vs. Floor Compliance

A category can be at floor-compliant levels while still being at Critical dependency concentration. Both conditions are tracked independently. Floor compliance addresses the quantity of domestic capacity; dependency concentration addresses the resilience of the supply chain. Both must be within bounds for a category to be classified as secure.

### AT2.4 — Foreign-Affiliate Routing

A supplier is treated as domestically controlled only when beneficial ownership, operational command, inventory disposition, patent or standards control, and essential-input routing are all subject to domestic law and Article VII audit. Routing supply through a domestic subsidiary, distributor, PBM, licensee, logistics arm, or standards body does not break foreign control if a foreign parent, affiliate, creditor, state-linked owner, or contractual veto can materially restrict supply, price, formulary access, repair parts, data access, or production inputs.

For dependency classification, such routed supply is attributed to the controlling foreign source. A domestic supplier that depends on a foreign affiliate for a survival-critical license, input, platform, or dispatch decision inherits that affiliate's concentration risk until the dependency is replaced, licensed on public-interest terms, or secured by treaty-enforceable fallback capacity.

## AT3 — Essential-Capacity Export Caps

### AT3.1 — Baseline Rule

Export of any good in an Essential Access category is permitted during normal operating conditions with no additional restriction beyond standard trade law. The protocol does not maintain export bans in normal times — this would contradict the abundance-through-trade principle and would harm both foreign partners and domestic producers.

### AT3.2 — Stress-Mode Export Caps

When any of the following conditions exist, automatic export caps activate for the relevant category:

| Trigger | Cap Level | Duration |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Category at Watch scarcity mode (Article VIII: < 21 days supply) | Exports capped at 50% of trailing 90-day export volume | Until category returns to Normal mode |
| Category at Scarcity mode (L2: < 14 days supply) | Exports suspended except under treaty obligation with reciprocal essential access guarantees | Until category returns to Watch or above |
| Category at Emergency mode (L3: < 7 days supply) | All exports suspended; existing export contracts may be fulfilled only from demonstrated surplus above CSM demand | Until category returns to Scarcity or above |
| Strategic floor breach | Exports suspended for breached category | Until floor is restored and verified |
| Critical dependency concentration | Export cap review required; no automatic cap, but export permits require oracle sign-off | Until dependency is reduced below Critical |

### AT3.3 — Foreign Humanitarian Impact Disclosure

The household-first duty stands: domestic Essential Access is protected before outward flow, and a domestic scarcity trigger never waits on foreign conditions. But the duty of mercy toward the foreign poor is procedural and visible, not merely declared.

Before an essential-category export cap activates against a counterparty whose population is import-dependent on that category, the Article III oracle network must publish the projected humanitarian effect of the cap on that population — including the categories affected, the dependent population's available alternative supply, and the projected time-to-shortage. Publication occurs through the Article VII transparency layer concurrently with cap activation and does not delay protection of the domestic floor.

The §AT5.5 humanitarian channel must be confirmed open before such a cap takes full effect against a dependent population. Where the channel is not open, the cap proceeds only with a concurrent, published plan to open or restore it. A cap that would foreseeably cause famine or comparable survival harm in a dependent foreign population may not be set tighter than the household-first duty requires, and the surplus-export and treaty provisions of §AT3.2 and §AT3.4 are read to preserve verified humanitarian flows wherever domestic survival is not thereby endangered.

### AT3.4 — Treaty Obligations

Export caps may not unilaterally void treaty obligations without the consent of the treaty partner except in Emergency (L3) conditions or verified foreign-supply weaponization (§AT5.3). Treaty obligations for essential goods must include a force majeure clause covering domestic scarcity events and a reciprocal essential-access guarantee — any treaty lacking these clauses may not be renewed under the protocol.

### AT3.5 — Cap Governance

Export cap activation and deactivation is automated against the Article III scarcity indices. Deliberative override of an automated cap requires Article VI ratification under the standard proposal pipeline with a mandatory 14-day review window. No executive or administrative body may unilaterally suspend an activated export cap except to tighten it further.

## AT4 — Foreign Capital Inflow Controls

### AT4.1 — Mandatory Flow Conversion

All foreign capital entering the protocol's economic territory converts to Flow at a published reference exchange rate at the point of entry. No foreign-currency-denominated assets, instruments, or accounts may circulate within the domestic economy as purchasing power.

### AT4.2 — Demurrage Treatment on Converted Capital

Converted foreign capital is subject to standard Flow demurrage rules (Article V §Flow idle-value rule) from the moment of conversion. There is no grace period, exemption, or reduced demurrage rate for foreign-origin capital. The purpose of this rule is to prevent the protocol's internal economy from becoming a destination for external capital seeking to escape demurrage regimes elsewhere — a dynamic that would recreate accumulation pressure from the outside even if internal accumulation is contained.

### AT4.3 — Investment Channel

Foreign-origin Flow that is committed to a registered productive investment project within 30 days of conversion enters the standard Article V investment channel and receives the standard protected-capital treatment for the duration of the project. The conversion clock stops when funds enter a registered, ring-fenced project escrow. Funds that do not enter a registered project within 30 days are subject to full idle demurrage retroactive to the conversion date.

### AT4.4 — Inflow Concentration Limits

No single foreign source may contribute more than 15% of total domestic Flow issuance in any rolling 12-month period. Above this threshold, additional inflows are subject to enhanced review and may be deferred or capped. This limit prevents a single foreign actor from acquiring effective macro-leverage over the domestic economy through capital position alone.

### AT4.5 — Outflow Rules

Flow may exit the domestic economy through legitimate trade settlement and treaty-governed payments. Flow export for the purpose of circumventing demurrage — converting to foreign currency to hold idle value — is treated as demurrage avoidance and subject to carrying charges. The Article VII transparency layer publishes aggregate inflow and outflow patterns quarterly.

## AT5 — The Reciprocity Membrane

### AT5.1 — Baseline Posture

The protocol's default trade posture is open. The protocol does not seek trade advantages, export subsidies, or preferential access that is not available to all counterparties on equivalent terms. It engages external economies as a cooperative partner and assumes good faith until evidence of extractive behavior is documented.

### AT5.2 — Reciprocity Classification

Trade relationships are assessed on a rolling basis against a published reciprocity standard. Assessment is a function of the Article VII environmental scanning mandate (Article VII §S4 function). A counterparty trade relationship may be classified as:

| Classification | Definition | Protocol Response |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cooperative** | Counterparty engages in good-faith exchange; no documented supply-chain weaponization, wage suppression targeting protocol sectors, or retaliatory barriers | Full open access; no additional constraints |
| **Contested** | Documented evidence of one or more: targeted wage suppression in traded-goods sectors competing with domestic production; retaliatory trade barriers in response to the protocol's governance structure; or supply-chain leverage threats | Enhanced monitoring; domestic producer support activated; diplomatic engagement required within 90 days |
| **Extractive** | Active weaponization of supply-chain dependency; deliberate disruption of Essential Access categories; financial attacks on the Flow system or its infrastructure | Graduated membrane response per §AT5.3 |

### AT5.3 — Graduated Membrane Response

When a counterparty is classified as Extractive, the membrane response escalates through the following stages. Each stage requires Article VI deliberative ratification:

**Stage 1 — Defensive posture:** export caps for all essential categories apply regardless of domestic scarcity mode. Enhanced border conversion requirements apply to all capital inflows. Dependency diversification for all categories sourced from the Extractive counterparty is mandated within 180 days.

**Stage 2 — Restricted access:** essential-category imports from the Extractive counterparty are subject to independent verification of origin, pricing, and supply-chain integrity before entry. Non-essential imports from the counterparty are subject to enhanced tariff-equivalent carrying charges. These carrying charges do not route to the general PFCR or to any domestic operating treasury. They route instead to a neutral, escrowed humanitarian and de-escalation fund, ring-fenced from general revenue and governed under Article VII transparency, whose permitted uses are limited to humanitarian channel support (§AT5.5), diversification of the dependencies that the Extractive classification exposed, and verified de-escalation toward reclassification. The protocol must hold no fiscal appetite to prolong an Extractive classification: the system must not profit from sustained hostility, and no domestic budget line may come to depend on a counterparty remaining classified Extractive. Any residue in the fund on reclassification to Cooperative is directed to humanitarian use, never to general revenue.

**Stage 3 — Suspension:** all import and export flows with the Extractive counterparty are suspended pending resolution. Existing contracts may be honored through verified third-party intermediaries only. Article VI must ratify Stage 3 with a two-thirds supermajority and a published resolution pathway.

### AT5.4 — Reclassification

Reclassification from Extractive to Contested or Cooperative requires verified behavioral change over a minimum 12-month observation window plus Article VI ratification. Reclassification cannot be triggered by diplomatic assurance alone; it requires oracle-verified evidence of changed practice.

### AT5.5 — Non-Retaliation Principle

The membrane response is defensive, not punitive. The protocol does not pursue economic coercion of foreign populations for the political behavior of their governments. Humanitarian trade — essential goods flowing to populations in need through verified non-governmental channels — is explicitly protected from membrane restrictions at all stages.

## AT6 — Trade Governance

### AT6.1 — Trade Oversight Body

Trade policy under this annex is governed by a standing Trade Stewardship Panel (TSP) operating under Article VI deliberative authority. The TSP is responsible for:

- Publishing and maintaining the counterparty reciprocity classification register
- Recommending strategic floor adjustments to the standard FAP process
- Reviewing and ratifying membrane stage escalations and de-escalations
- Commissioning the Article VII supply-chain concentration audits
- Publishing quarterly trade-health reports for the Article VII transparency layer

### AT6.2 — Panel Composition

The TSP consists of nine members serving staggered three-year terms. Composition requirements:
- Three members drawn from Article III oracle network (measurement expertise)
- Two members drawn from Article VI civic deliberation (democratic accountability)
- Two independent economists with no financial interest in the protocol's traded-goods sectors
- One representative of domestic essential-goods producers
- One representative of organized labor in traded-goods sectors

No TSP member may hold a financial interest in any enterprise that benefits materially from a specific trade classification decision during their term. Conflict-of-interest violations are referred to the Federated Ombuds (Annex AI).

### AT6.3 — Relationship to Article V

Trade governance does not create a separate economic authority. All Flow issuance, investment channels, and capital controls remain under Article V architecture. The TSP operates as a specialized deliberative body within the Article VI structure, with authority limited to the matters defined in this annex.

### AT6.4 — Treaty Negotiation

Trade treaties are negotiated by the TSP in consultation with affected Article VI constituencies and ratified by a two-thirds Article VI supermajority before taking effect. No treaty may override the strategic floor definitions (§AT1), the export cap triggers (§AT3), or the mandatory Flow conversion requirement (§AT4.1). Any treaty clause that purports to override these is void on its face.

### AT6.5 — Essential-Sector Conglomerate Operating Rule

Essential-sector conglomerates — including oil, gas, grid, medicine, logistics, PBM-style medicine-access intermediaries, and critical infrastructure firms — may operate inside the protocol economy only as producers, maintainers, distributors, researchers, or regulated utility stewards. They may earn Flow for verified production, reliability, transition work, innovation, and public-interest delivery. They may not convert control over fuel, grids, medicines, patents, logistics, data, capital, standards bodies, or foreign affiliates into survival leverage, rule authority, enforcement privilege, or exemption from demurrage and beneficial-ownership review.

A **survival-leverage actor** is any public, private, domestic, foreign, or affiliate-routed entity whose refusal, delay, pricing condition, patent assertion, formulary decision, logistics control, standards intervention, data denial, financing condition, or litigation posture can materially impair an Essential Access category, CSM-designated medicine, strategic floor, or Article IV survival guarantee. Survival-leverage status is functional, not formal: ownership form, domicile, contractor tier, or market label cannot remove it.

The Trade Stewardship Panel must maintain a sector-by-sector refusal plan for every Essential Access category exposed to conglomerate leverage. Each plan must identify: largest-supplier exit impact; reserve drawdown duration; fallback public, cooperative, treaty, or independent private capacity; beneficial-owner trace completeness; and lobbying/capture exposure. The [Conglomerate Transition Dossier](../governance/Conglomerate_Transition_Dossier.md) supplies the evidence and test format.

No essential-sector firm may be treated as non-critical merely because it is formally private, foreign, or operating through affiliates. If a survival-leverage actor refuses lawful supply, imposes survival-hostage terms, withholds operational data, disables essential infrastructure, blocks transfer to a compliant operator, or causes a verified floor breach, public receiver authority may activate for the affected function only. Receiver authority is temporary, reviewable, compensated at constitutional public-use standards, and limited to continuity of service, reserve protection, asset maintenance, and transition to compliant public, cooperative, treaty, or independent private capacity.

For CSM-designated medicines, compulsory licensing and public or cooperative manufacturing activate when patent, exclusivity, PBM, formulary, foreign-affiliate, API-input, or manufacturing control creates a verified shortage risk, floor breach, unjustified access denial, or refusal to supply on published public-interest terms. Compensation must be lawful and transparent, but no exclusivity claim may override the CSM floor. Activation, rates, and deactivation are published through Article VII and tested against the Conglomerate Transition Dossier refusal drill.

### AT6.6 — Compliant Alternative Supplier Pre-Registration (CASP)

**CASP is a mandatory pre-condition, not a post-refusal remedy.**

Before any essential-sector procurement contract can be renewed or extended beyond its initial term, the commissioning authority must maintain a register of at least two independently capable compliant alternative suppliers for each essential category (food, fuel, medicine, grid, logistics). The register must include:

- Verified production capacity sufficient to cover the CSM floor for the relevant category for at least 90 days without the primary supplier
- Pre-committed procurement contracts (not letters of intent) with each registered alternative supplier, with automatic activation clauses triggered by: (a) a Watch-level concentration finding for the primary supplier under AT6.2, or (b) the primary supplier issuing a refusal notice, or (c) the primary supplier failing to deliver against an existing contract for more than 14 consecutive days, or (d) the primary supplier entering a degradation period as defined below
- Beneficial-ownership tracing confirming that no registered alternative is controlled by the same natural persons or state actors as the primary supplier
- Annual capacity verification by an inspector independent of both the primary and alternative suppliers
- A co-signed service-level baseline established at the time of pre-registration, specifying minimum acceptable performance on at least four measurable dimensions (e.g., delivery timeliness, fill rate, quality certification rate, and incident response time) and the contracted price corridor

**Degradation trigger (clause d):** A primary supplier enters a degradation period when it performs below the co-signed service-level baseline on two or more measurable dimensions simultaneously for 30 or more consecutive days, confirmed by at least two independent sources (which may include the commissioning authority's own audited measurement, an independent logistics auditor, or a verified oracle node). Upon confirmation of a degradation period, the commissioning authority must notify CASP-registered alternatives within 7 days and prepare for contract activation within 30 days if performance does not recover. A degradation period that extends beyond 60 days without recovery automatically triggers CASP activation under clause (d).

**Price corridor:** Contracts with CASP-registered suppliers must specify a price corridor of ±15% of the registered pricing index for the relevant category, adjusted quarterly for verified regional inflation. Pricing outside the corridor is not automatically prohibited but requires public explanation and enhanced review. Price corridor violations that persist for two consecutive quarters are treated as a contract defect and trigger re-registration review.

**Activation is automatic, not discretionary.** When a trigger condition is met (as above), pre-committed contracts with CASP-registered suppliers activate without requiring a new political decision. The public receiver authority remains available as a fallback if CASP-registered alternatives also fail or refuse, but CASP is the primary mitigation.

**CASP exemptions:** A commissioning authority may apply for a CASP hardship exemption if: (a) fewer than two independently capable alternative suppliers exist for a category in the relevant geography, and (b) the authority publishes a written diversification plan with a binding 24-month timeline for developing at least one alternative supplier. The exemption is reviewed quarterly by the Capture Dashboard and lapses automatically if the diversification plan falls behind schedule.

## AT7 — Threat Surface and Failure Modes

### AT7.1 — Strategic Floor Capture

**Failure mode:** the floor-setting process is captured by domestic producers who inflate floor definitions to guarantee protected markets, or by foreign interests who lobby to suppress floor levels to create dependency.

**Patches:** floor definitions are anchored to the CSM basket (Article Y) and Article III physical measurements, not to producer or importer preferences. Floor adjustments require H-2 supermajority for any CSM-touching category. TSP conflict-of-interest rules apply to all floor discussions.

### AT7.2 — Reciprocity Classification Weaponization

**Failure mode:** the Extractive classification is weaponized as a political tool against counterparties that are economically fair but politically disagreeable, or is suppressed through diplomatic pressure against economically extractive counterparties with political leverage.

**Patches:** classification criteria are oracle-verified and evidence-based, not TSP-discretionary. Classifications are published and subject to public challenge through the standard dispute mechanism. Suppression of a documented Extractive classification by any TSP member is a conflict-of-interest violation referred to the Federated Ombuds.

### AT7.3 — Export Cap Circumvention

**Failure mode:** export caps are circumvented through re-labeling, transshipment through third countries, or financial instruments that achieve the economic effect of export without triggering the monitoring system.

**Patches:** export caps are applied at the commodity level, not the contract level. Transshipment through a third country that has a Cooperative classification does not exempt goods from origin-based cap calculations. Financial instruments that represent a claim on capped domestic production are treated as constructive exports.

### AT7.4 — Capital Inflow Laundering

**Failure mode:** foreign capital enters the Flow system through a chain of domestic entities to avoid the 30-day conversion clock and beneficial ownership disclosure requirements, achieving effective demurrage-free accumulation inside the protocol.

**Patches:** beneficial ownership tracing applies to the full corporate structure of any entity receiving converted foreign capital. Flow received by a domestic entity from a foreign-origin source within 12 months of the original conversion is subject to retroactive demurrage if the investment channel requirements were not met at the time of conversion. The Article VII transparency layer publishes aggregate beneficial-ownership concentration data quarterly.

### AT7.5 — Membrane Ratchet

**Failure mode:** once a counterparty is classified as Extractive and Stage 2 or 3 membrane responses are activated, vested interests in the restrictions form domestically (import-competing producers, labor groups), making reclassification politically impossible even when the counterparty's behavior genuinely changes.

**Patches:** reclassification criteria are evidence-based and the 12-month observation window is mandatory but bounded — the TSP may not extend the observation window indefinitely without Article VI ratification. The non-retaliation principle (§AT5.5) creates a structural pressure toward resolution. Foreign populations affected by Stage 3 restrictions have an explicit humanitarian exemption that limits the collateral pressure domestically.
