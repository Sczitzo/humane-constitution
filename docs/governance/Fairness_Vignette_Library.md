# Fairness Vignette Library

**Purpose:** To test the Humane Constitution against real human lives — not ideal citizens, but people living in hard circumstances. Each vignette asks whether the system's guarantees hold under realistic pressure, where the constitution is silent or vague, and what the Christ-centered evaluation requires. This library extends the four personas (Marcus, Sasha, Leo, Janelle) from the initial ChatGPT fairness audit, which correctly identified risks around Service Record abuse and demurrage but missed that the constitution already has a hardship-safe Voice floor and a one-person-one-vote civic floor.

**Governing question:** Does this person receive Essential Access, baseline Voice, and protection from bureaucratic exclusion — not because they have earned it, but because they are human?

**Governing sentence from the Christ-Centered Evaluation:** *The Humane Constitution must serve humanity under God; it must never become humanity's substitute for God.*

---

## How to Read a Vignette

Each persona is structured as:

- **Who they are** — the situation
- **What the constitution guarantees** — specific clauses that apply
- **What is underspecified or at risk** — where the language is vague, the mechanism is missing, or abuse is possible
- **The Christ-centered question** — which of the eight evaluative questions is most at stake
- **Verdict** — strong, conditional, or weak, with the required fix

A **strong** verdict means the constitution clearly covers this person and the delivery mechanism is adequate. A **conditional** verdict means the guarantee exists but implementation gaps create real risk. A **weak** verdict means this person could fall through a gap that is not yet closed.

## Constitutional Provisions Referenced Throughout

- **Article IV (Survival):** Essential Access is unconditional; it cannot be sold, pledged, or withdrawn as punishment. Physical cash remains available. Pre-existing care networks (food pantries, mutual aid, religious outreach) are explicitly supported and not displaced.
- **Article VI (Voice, Service Record & Public Decisions):** Voice is based on recent verified contribution *plus hardship-safe floor rules*. Protected Pause preserves continuity during verified hardship (disability, caregiving, age, crisis, illness, disaster). Recognized-contribution audit required every two years. Constitutional matters stay one-person-one-vote. No single contribution sector may dominate civic weighting.
- **Article I (Invariants):** Survival is unconditional. Human worth is not measured. No coercion by Voice, Service Record, or Flow for basic rights.
- **Article V (Markets, Commons & Public Finance):** Demurrage applies to large idle balances, not survival savings. Operating-float exemption exists. Physical cash remains available. Essential Access cannot be converted to money.

## Group 1: Capacity and Disability

### Vignette 1 — Miriam

**Who she is.** Miriam is 38 years old. She has ALS — amyotrophic lateral sclerosis — and has been non-verbal for two years. She communicates through an eye-tracking device when she has energy. She cannot perform any formal service now or in the future. She lives with her sister, who provides full-time care.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Essential Access is unconditional. Article IV states: "baseline essentials are guaranteed and cannot be withdrawn as punishment." Miriam's inability to contribute in any registered way cannot reduce her Essential Access.
- Voice has a hardship-safe floor. Article VI states Voice is based on contribution *plus* hardship-safe floor rules. Miriam qualifies for the floor, not zero Voice.
- Protected Pause applies. Article VI lists "disability" explicitly as a life circumstance that changes expectations and triggers continuity protection.
- Constitutional matters stay one-person-one-vote. Miriam's vote on constitutional questions is equal to anyone else's.
- Her sister's caregiving can count as recognized contribution under Article VI's explicit recognition of "care, maintenance... and similar work."

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- How does Miriam exercise her baseline Voice if the primary interface is digital and her eye-tracking device is not integrated? The constitution requires physical cash alternatives for Flow but does not specify accessible alternative participation channels for Voice and civic deliberation.
- Who verifies her Protected Pause status? If she cannot advocate for herself and her sister is also her verification contact, a biased or negligent local administrator could claim the pause is expired or unverified.
- The constitution does not specify a minimum Voice floor amount. "Hardship-safe floor" exists, but what it means in practice — one unit, half the average, some percentage — is left to implementation. If the floor is nominal, Miriam's civic personhood is present in name only.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 3: *Does this protect the vulnerable, the poor, the outcast, and the burdened?* (Matt 25:35–40; Luke 4:18–19). Jesus identifies himself with "the least of these" — those who cannot advocate for themselves, who depend entirely on the faithfulness of others. The test is whether the system is designed so that Miriam does not need to fight for what was already promised.

**Verdict: Conditional.** The guarantee exists. The mechanism is insufficient. Required fixes: (1) Specify accessible participation alternatives for persons who cannot use digital interfaces. (2) Require independent third-party verification for Protected Pause when the person cannot self-advocate. (3) Define the Voice floor as a minimum percentage of the standard allocation — not symbolic, but genuinely participatory.

### Vignette 2 — Daniel

**Who he is.** Daniel is 29 years old. He has schizophrenia. In stable periods, he works part-time at a community garden, attends civic deliberation, and has a modest Service Record. During episodes, he is hospitalized for weeks at a time, sometimes involuntarily. Over the past three years, he has had two hospitalizations totaling eleven weeks. His Service Record shows gaps. He has lost civic eligibility for a neighborhood review panel because his record showed "insufficient recent contribution."

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Essential Access continues unconditionally. Hospitalization does not end it.
- Protected Pause applies. "Illness" is an explicit Protected Pause trigger. His gaps during hospitalization should be protected, not counted against him.
- His recognized contributions during stable periods (community garden, civic work) can count.
- Disability is listed as a factor that "can change expectations."

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- Whether Protected Pause must be proactively claimed (and by whom, when Daniel is hospitalized and cannot claim it himself) or is automatically triggered by verified hospitalization is not specified. If it requires Daniel to file a claim, he will miss it during every episode by definition.
- "Insufficient recent contribution" as a disqualification from civic roles does not appear to have a mental-health exception built in. The constitution says disability "can change expectations" but does not mandate a constitutional carve-out from eligibility rules for roles that require "recent" contribution.
- The two-year contribution audit (Article VI) must explicitly test whether episodic mental illness is being systematically excluded from civic roles. Without this named check, administrators may not look for it.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 4: *Does this honor truth, mercy, justice, forgiveness, and reconciliation?* (Matt 18:21–35). Mercy does not punish a person for a condition they did not choose. A system that counts Daniel's hospitalizations as civic failures has confused the symptom with the person.

**Verdict: Conditional.** The framework is sympathetic but the gap is real. Required fixes: (1) Protected Pause must be automatically triggered by verified hospitalization — no self-filing required during incapacity. A designated advocate or the hospital system must be able to register the pause. (2) Civic role eligibility rules must contain an explicit episodic-disability exception: gaps caused by verified illness do not count as contribution failures. (3) The biennial contribution audit must specifically test whether people with episodic mental illness are being excluded at higher rates.

## Group 2: Legal and Institutional Status

### Vignette 3 — Ray

**Who he is.** Ray is 44 years old. He is serving an 8-year sentence for armed robbery. He has been incarcerated for three years. Before incarceration, he had a modest Flow income and a small Service Record from a community cleanup program. During his incarceration, he teaches reading to other inmates — unregistered, informal, unpaid.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Essential Access is not conditioned on law-abiding status. Article IV makes no exception for incarcerated persons. Ray should receive Essential Access within the facility.
- The one-person-one-vote civic floor applies to constitutional matters. Whether Ray can vote on constitutional questions while incarcerated is not addressed.
- Survival cannot be withdrawn as punishment. This would seem to prevent using Essential Access reduction as a disciplinary tool within the facility.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- The constitution is silent on whether incarcerated persons retain Voice. Most societies suspend civic rights during incarceration. The constitution does not address this, leaving it to implementation — which creates a capture risk where prison administrators or political actors could strip civic standing during incarceration without constitutional basis.
- Ray's informal teaching inside the prison cannot be registered in any current mechanism. He earns no Service Record for genuine contribution. When he is released, he re-enters with a gap.
- Essential Access delivery inside a prison facility depends on the facility cooperating with the system's delivery infrastructure. If the prison is not enrolled, Ray receives no Essential Access even though the constitution says it is unconditional. This is the same gap as Amara (Vignette 4) — the delivery mechanism does not yet reach everyone the guarantee covers.
- The constitution's affirmative obligation clause (Article IV footnote): jurisdictions that have not joined the system must still be worked toward — the commitment is not satisfied by the gap. This applies to prison facilities not enrolled in Essential Access delivery.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 3: *Does this protect the vulnerable, the poor, the outcast, and the burdened?* Jesus visited prisons metaphorically in Matthew 25:36 ("I was in prison and you came to me") and literally through his own arrest and execution. A system that loses people at the prison gate has failed exactly where Jesus asked us not to.

**Verdict: Conditional to Weak.** Essential Access should cover Ray but the delivery mechanism may not reach him. Voice status during incarceration is constitutionally unresolved. Required fixes: (1) The constitution should explicitly state that incarceration does not end Essential Access; the delivery obligation falls on the state. (2) Informal contribution inside institutions (teaching, care, mediation) must have a path to Service Record recognition. (3) The constitutional position on civic rights during incarceration must be stated — if rights are suspended, there must be a restoration path upon release, not a permanent gap.

### Vignette 4 — Amara

**Who she is.** Amara is 31 years old. She crossed the border illegally eight years ago, fleeing gang violence in her home country. She works as a domestic worker. She lives in a city that has not consented to the Humane Constitution system. She has no identity documentation recognized by the system. She is not enrolled in Essential Access, has no Voice, and has no Service Record.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Article IV contains an affirmative obligation clause: "The dignity floor is not contingent on membership; its delivery mechanism is a work in progress that this system is obligated to complete." Participating jurisdictions must allocate 2% of PFCR capacity toward cross-boundary dignity floor delivery in non-consenting adjacent communities.
- The constitution prohibits survival coercion. Amara is subject to survival coercion every day — she works for fear of losing housing, food, or deportation.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- The 2% allocation requirement is a future obligation, not a present delivery mechanism. Amara receives nothing today under this clause.
- Identity verification is required for Essential Access enrollment. The constitution requires "one person, one core wallet" to prevent duplicates. Amara has no documentation. She cannot enroll. The constitution does not specify a documentation-free enrollment path.
- Physical cash and offline alternatives for Flow are required, but these require an enrolled identity. No enrollment, no access.
- The "pre-existing care networks" clause (Article IV) protects and supports mutual aid groups and religious food pantries — which Amara may actually be using. This is the most relevant protection for her right now. The constitution must not displace these.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 3: *Does this protect the vulnerable, the poor, the outcast, and the burdened?* And question 1: *Does this reflect love of neighbor?* The Good Samaritan (Luke 10:25–37) did not check documentation before helping the man in the ditch. A system that requires papers before delivering survival essentials has built a bureaucratic condition into what it calls an unconditional floor.

**Verdict: Weak for now, conditional in the long term.** Required fixes: (1) A documentation-free emergency enrollment path is constitutionally necessary — where identity cannot be verified through normal channels, community attestation, biometric registration without citizenship linkage, or similar alternatives must exist. (2) The 2% cross-boundary obligation must have a timeline and enforcement mechanism, not just an aspiration. (3) The pre-existing care network protection must be operationalized so that mutual aid groups serving Amara can receive system support without requiring her enrollment.

## Group 3: Religious and Cultural Opt-Out

### Vignette 5 — The Yoder Community

**Who they are.** A close-knit religious community of 340 people who have explicitly refused to join the system on grounds of religious conscience. They do not use digital technology, do not accept government-issued identity cards, and do not participate in civic deliberation outside their congregation. They grow their own food, build their own homes, educate their own children, and provide mutual care within the community. They are not hostile — they simply want to be left alone before God.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Article IV requires that "pre-existing community networks that provide survival essentials: mutual aid groups, religious food pantries, family support structures, neighborhood care networks, and congregational outreach" be actively supported and not displaced.
- Freedom of conscience before God is an Article I protected commitment: "Persons, families, congregations, and voluntary communities retain protected space to seek, worship, obey, and serve God according to conscience."
- The exit rule (FC-120, FC-121) allows communities to leave or never join by two-thirds vote with a 730-day transition. Essential Access continues during transition.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- If the Yoder Community never joined, the exit rule may not apply — it governs departure, not non-enrollment. Their status as a community that was never inside the system is not addressed.
- The "actively support and not displace" clause is aspirational. How the system delivers concrete support to a community that refuses its infrastructure is not specified. If "support" means "offer enrollment they will refuse," it is not support.
- The constitution's capacity measurement and issuance systems require participation to function. A non-participating community may be invisible to capacity calculations, affecting neighboring communities' resource allocations.
- If a member of the Yoder Community experiences a genuine survival emergency and needs medical care beyond the community's ability to provide, the system has no clear path to help them without requiring enrollment.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 6: *Does this resist Babel-like temptations — pride, domination, coercive unity, and the desire to build a system that replaces dependence on God?* (Gen 11:1–9). A system that says "we guarantee your dignity floor — unless you refuse our infrastructure" has made the system the mediator of survival. The Yoder Community's refusal is a test of whether the system's guarantees are truly unconditional or contingent on participation in the system's own machinery.

**Verdict: Conditional.** The values align but the mechanism gaps are real. Required fixes: (1) Define a "non-enrollment accommodation" status — communities that never joined are not treated as exited, but as adjacent communities under the cross-boundary delivery obligation. (2) Emergency access must be available to non-enrolled persons without requiring full enrollment — one-time documented delivery that does not force ongoing participation. (3) The pre-existing care network support must include funding pathways that reach non-enrolled communities through third-party intermediaries they trust (churches, humanitarian organizations).

### Vignette 6 — Father Emmanuel

**Who he is.** Father Emmanuel is 56 years old. He is a Catholic priest living under vows of celibacy, obedience, and poverty. He earns no market income. He owns nothing personally. He serves his parish full-time: celebrating Mass, hearing confessions, visiting the sick and dying, counseling families in crisis, running a food pantry that distributes essential goods to two hundred families per week, mentoring at-risk youth. He does not register these activities in any civic service system. He has never filed a Service Record claim. He receives a modest stipend from his diocese.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Article VI explicitly recognizes "spiritual leadership in the community" and "the patient work of raising children and caring for the elderly" as contributions that "must not be systematically excluded simply because they are harder to verify."
- The biennial contribution audit must test whether "contributions that sustain community but resist easy measurement" are being included.
- Essential Access covers him at the hardship-safe floor level because he has no market income.
- His food pantry is a "pre-existing care network" explicitly protected and supported under Article IV.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- Nothing requires Father Emmanuel to register his service. But without registration, he has no Service Record. If Service Record becomes a prerequisite for civic roles he might be called to (community ethics panels, mediation boards, emergency oversight), his entire lifetime of service is constitutionally invisible.
- The biennial audit must catch this, but audits correct systemic gaps — they do not create Service Record entries retroactively.
- His diocese stipend is Flow income, subject to normal rules. As long as it is below the demurrage threshold, no problem. But if the diocese receives significant Flow through the public banking system, demurrage routing could affect the diocese's ability to maintain the food pantry.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 2: *Does this serve people rather than exalt the system?* (Mark 10:42–45). Father Emmanuel's entire life is service. A system that does not see him as a contributor because his service does not flow through the system's recognition channels has confused the record with the reality.

**Verdict: Conditional — leaning strong.** The constitutional text is actually generous here, but the recognition mechanism requires action. Required fixes: (1) The contribution recognition framework must include a clergy/ministry registration pathway that is opt-in, low-barrier, and does not require ongoing digital reporting. (2) The food pantry's pre-existing network protection must include a financial support pathway that does not require the pantry to become an enrolled Essential Access delivery node. (3) The biennial audit should specifically flag spiritual care and religious ministry as a category that resists measurement, with published findings.

## Group 4: Informal and Invisible Labor

### Vignette 7 — Rosa

**Who she is.** Rosa is 67 years old. Her daughter died of an overdose two years ago, leaving three grandchildren aged 4, 7, and 11. Rosa is their full-time caregiver. She does not work in any market. She does not engage in formal civic service. She receives a Flow pension from her late husband's employment years. She spends her days cooking, driving children to school and medical appointments, helping with homework, managing trauma, and holding the family together.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Article VI explicitly names caregiving as recognized contribution: "care, maintenance, safety, education... and similar work can count when verified."
- The biennial audit must test whether caregiving "that sustains community but resists easy measurement" is being included.
- "The patient work of raising children" is specifically named in the audit mandate as a contribution that "must not be systematically excluded."
- Protected Pause covers her age as a factor that "can change expectations."
- Her pension is Flow income; as long as it is below the operating-float exemption, demurrage does not apply.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- Caregiving "can count when verified." What does verification mean for Rosa? She has no employer to confirm her service, no organization to attest to it. She cares for her own grandchildren, not for a registered care institution. The verification pathway for informal family caregiving is not specified.
- If Rosa does not claim her caregiving as contribution — because she does not know she can, because the process is burdensome, or because she does not have the digital literacy to navigate it — she receives no Service Record credit. The constitution does not create an automatic recognition mechanism; it requires action from Rosa.
- Her Voice allocation at the hardship-safe floor may be higher than zero but meaningfully lower than an active contributor. Given that she is raising three children who are part of the community, her stake in local decisions is high but her Voice weight may not reflect it.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 5: *Does this preserve human dignity rather than reducing people to data, utility, productivity, or compliance?* Rosa's value to her grandchildren, her neighborhood, and God is not captured in any system metric. The question is whether the system is humble enough to acknowledge that.

**Verdict: Conditional.** The values are right. The recognition mechanism for informal family caregiving is underbuilt. Required fixes: (1) Family caregiving must have a light-documentation verification path — school enrollment records, medical appointment records, and child welfare system contact can together confirm caregiving without requiring Rosa to prove she is doing what she is obviously doing. (2) The biennial audit must specifically test whether grandparent and kinship caregivers are receiving contribution recognition at rates proportional to their actual contribution.

### Vignette 8 — The Kahananui Family

**Who they are.** The Kahananui family is an extended indigenous family of eighteen adults living on ancestral land. They practice subsistence agriculture, fishing, and traditional craft. They exchange goods within their community and occasionally with neighboring communities through barter and gift. They have enrolled in the system minimally — they have identity wallets for Essential Access — but they rarely use Flow, do not participate in the digital civic interface, and maintain their traditional governance structures for decisions within their community.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Essential Access covers them. They have enrolled.
- Physical cash and offline alternatives for Flow are constitutionally required — they are not excluded from market participation simply because they prefer not to use the digital system.
- Their traditional governance is not displaced by Article VI — the civic floor applies to system-level decisions, not internal community governance.
- Their barter and gift exchanges within the community are not denominated in Flow. Article V's anti-conversion rules prevent the system from treating their internal economy as Flow-equivalent.
- Pre-existing care networks are protected.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- Demurrage applies to Flow balances. Because the Kahananui family rarely uses Flow, they are unlikely to accumulate balances that trigger demurrage. But if a family member receives Flow from the system (a service payment, an infrastructure contract), and they hold it in an account while deciding how to use it, they could be hit by demurrage on a balance they are not actively "hoarding" — they are simply outside the financial rhythm the system assumes.
- Their traditional governance does not produce a Service Record. Their elder council, their dispute resolution practices, their ecological stewardship — none of it is registered. They are constitutionally invisible as contributors despite carrying enormous community responsibility.
- The biennial contribution audit is required to find this gap. But the audit is designed to find patterns; the Kahananui family may be too small a population to appear as a statistically significant exclusion.
- The indigenous land status is not addressed in the constitution. Their ancestral land claim and the system's use-rights framework (Article V: "housing and land are governed as use-rights or leases") could conflict if the system does not explicitly recognize pre-existing indigenous tenure.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 6: *Does this resist Babel-like temptations — coercive unity, the desire to build a system that replaces dependence on God?* Indigenous communities have experienced centuries of systems that "guaranteed" their welfare while dismantling their way of life. The test is not whether the system is well-intentioned but whether it leaves the Kahananui family genuinely free.

**Verdict: Conditional.** The Essential Access and physical cash provisions are strong. The Service Record and indigenous tenure gaps are real. Required fixes: (1) The contribution recognition framework must have a pathway for traditional ecological stewardship, customary governance, and community knowledge-keeping to count as Service Record. (2) The use-rights framework must explicitly preserve pre-existing indigenous land tenure as a recognized form of use-right, not a challenge to it. (3) The biennial audit must include methodology for identifying excluded communities too small to show up in aggregate data.

## Group 5: Privacy and Safety

### Vignette 9 — Elena

**Who she is.** Elena is 34 years old. She has fled a domestic violence situation. Her abuser knows her name, her previous address, her workplace, and her social connections. She has moved to a different city and is living in a shelter. The identity verification system requires a permanent wallet tied to her legal identity. Her abuser has connections in the local administration and has previously used her legal name to track her.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Essential Access delivery is unconditional. Elena should receive it.
- Article IV protects privacy: "Essential Access cannot be sold, pledged, collateralized, or informally monetized." The system is not supposed to be a surveillance tool.
- Physical cash and offline alternatives for Flow mean she is not required to use the digital system for basic transactions.
- The "coercion-specific penalties" and "safe-report channels" for Essential Access coercion are relevant — her situation is exactly the coercion the system is designed to prevent.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- The "one person, one core wallet" requirement prevents duplicate claims — but it also creates a permanent link between Elena's identity and her wallet. If her abuser or a corrupt administrator can access wallet location data, she is findable.
- The constitution does not specify an address-confidential enrollment option. Domestic violence survivors routinely need to access services without disclosing their location. The wallet system must support this.
- If Elena cannot use her legal identity safely, can she access Essential Access at all? The system's integrity mechanism (one wallet per person) is designed to prevent fraud, but it creates a dangerous tension with safety for people whose legal identity is a vector of harm.
- The constitution's privacy provisions for Article VII dashboards mention "aggregation thresholds" and "small-cell suppression" — but this is about public reporting, not individual wallet privacy. The individual-level privacy protections for Essential Access delivery are not specified.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 3: *Does this protect the vulnerable, the poor, the outcast, and the burdened?* And question 4: *Does this honor truth, mercy, justice?* Elena's protection from an abuser is a justice matter. A system that cannot separate identity verification (needed for integrity) from location disclosure (dangerous for her) has failed to design for the lives of the vulnerable.

**Verdict: Weak on the specific safety gap.** The overall intent is right. Required fixes: (1) A confidential enrollment option — where the wallet is linked to legal identity in a sealed record accessible only under court order, and all location data is suppressed from administrators — must be constitutionally specified. (2) Emergency Essential Access without wallet linkage should be possible for persons in documented safety situations, with a time-limited window for full enrollment under confidential terms. (3) The abuse-reporting channel must include a pathway for reporting administrator misuse of wallet data.

## Group 6: Age and Transition

### Vignette 10 — Tomás

**Who he is.** Tomás is 15 years old. He lives with his mother. He is one year from civic majority. His mother receives Essential Access for both of them. He attends school, volunteers at a community library, and has begun attending civic deliberation sessions as an observer. He wants to understand the system he is about to enter.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Essential Access covers Tomás as a dependent. Article IV's caregiver transfer provisions allow his mother to receive Essential Access on his behalf.
- At majority, Tomás will receive his own wallet and baseline Voice allocation.
- His library volunteering may be registrable as contribution, potentially beginning a Service Record before majority.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- The transition from dependent coverage to individual enrollment is not specified. If there is no automatic transition — if Tomás must proactively apply at 16 — he could experience a gap.
- The age of civic majority is not specified in the constitution. It says "persons" without defining when personhood-for-constitutional-purposes begins. Most societies use 18; some use 16. The choice matters for Voice, voting on constitutional matters, and Service Record eligibility.
- Can a 15-year-old begin accumulating a Service Record? If yes, what protections exist against exploiting youth contribution for adult administrative benefit? If no, his volunteering creates no lasting civic record.
- The biennial contribution audit must eventually ask: are young people finding meaningful pathways into civic life, or is the system opaque to them until they turn 18?

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 5: *Does this preserve human dignity rather than reducing people to data, utility, productivity, or compliance?* Children are not pre-adults waiting to become contributing members of society. They are persons now, with dignity now, learning what it means to live with others. The system should welcome their formation, not just their eventual enrollment.

**Verdict: Conditional.** The coverage is there; the transition mechanism is not. Required fixes: (1) Automatic wallet enrollment at civic majority — no required filing by the newly-adult person. (2) Define civic majority explicitly in the constitution or a named annex. (3) Youth contribution recognition should be possible with age-appropriate protections: lighter verification requirements and no Service Record use for non-youth-facing civic roles until adult majority.

### Vignette 11 — Margaret

**Who she is.** Margaret is 84 years old. Her husband died three years ago. She lives alone in her home. She can no longer perform any physical service. She has mild cognitive decline — she participates in conversations but sometimes loses track. Her Service Record is from decades of community involvement that has now ended. She is afraid of losing her standing in the community.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Essential Access is unconditional. Her age does not reduce it.
- Voice has a hardship-safe floor. "Age" is explicitly listed as a life circumstance that changes expectations. Margaret's Voice cannot go to zero on grounds of her non-contribution.
- Protected Pause applies. Her decline is ongoing, not a temporary episode.
- Her decades of contribution are her Service Record history; the constitution does not specify that Service Records expire, though Voice fades.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- Protected Pause appears to be designed for temporary disruptions (illness, crisis, disaster). Margaret's situation is permanent decline — she will not return to active contribution. The mechanism must accommodate permanent protected status without requiring repeated re-verification that she is still declining.
- Her cognitive decline may affect her ability to navigate the digital wallet system. Essential Access delivery should not require active digital interaction that she can no longer reliably perform. The physical cash alternative is constitutionally available but requires her to access it.
- Her fear of losing standing is valid: if people around her equate reduced Voice with reduced worth, the social culture can undermine what the constitution tries to protect. The constitution prohibits the system from ranking persons, but it cannot prohibit the human tendency to do so informally.
- The biennial audit should test whether elderly persons with cognitive decline are receiving their Essential Access reliably and their Voice floor without requiring active maintenance.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 3: *Does this protect the vulnerable?* And question 7: *Does this produce good fruit in practice?* The fruit test for Margaret is simple: does she live her final years with dignity, security, and a genuine sense of belonging — or does she spend them anxious about bureaucratic maintenance of her status?

**Verdict: Strong in principle, conditional in practice.** Required fixes: (1) Protected Pause for permanent conditions should be a one-time designation, renewed only if the person's condition materially changes, not on a fixed cycle. (2) Essential Access delivery must include non-digital pathways (physical delivery, designated trusted contact, community navigator) for persons with cognitive or physical limitations. (3) The contribution audit must specifically examine whether the oldest cohort is receiving their floor protections reliably.

## Group 7: Wealth Routing and Gaming

### Vignette 12 — Victor

**Who he is.** Victor is 52 years old. He is a successful entrepreneur. He has accumulated substantial Flow through his businesses over fifteen years. To minimize demurrage exposure, his legal team has structured his holdings into: five time-bound project escrows (infrastructure and real estate developments), two term-finance pools backing regional agricultural cooperatives, one catastrophe reserve for his main company, and a collection of Flow-denominated equity positions in worker-cooperative enterprises. On paper, none of his balances are "idle." In practice, the development timelines are indefinitely extensible, the cooperatives are shells, and the equity positions are structured to appreciate without requiring active management.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Demurrage applies to large idle balances above the operating-float exemption.
- Protected capital treatment (project escrows, term-finance pools, catastrophe reserves) exists but requires "preregistered, ring-fenced, milestone-reviewed by independent auditors, and subject to reversion if purpose drifts or deadlines become circular."
- "Deadlines become circular" is the key phrase — the constitution anticipates this exact structure.
- Anti-hoarding, anti-monopoly, and anti-rent-seeking rules apply to all Flow.
- High-value asset registries must be transparent.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- "Milestone-reviewed by independent auditors" is required, but who appoints these auditors, how independent they must be, and what happens when milestones are repeatedly extended is not fully specified. Victor's legal team can likely find auditors willing to certify indefinitely-extended timelines.
- "Purpose drifts" is undefined. A real estate project that takes twelve years instead of four has drifted, but the constitution does not specify when drift triggers reversion.
- The "worker-cooperative enterprises" that are shells pass the structural test (they are cooperatives, which the constitution prefers) but fail the purpose test. The constitution does not have a mechanism for detecting phantom cooperatives created to absorb wealth without delivering cooperative benefit.
- Wealth can be preserved not through Flow accumulation but through influence over the decision-making bodies that interpret the rules — which is the capture risk the constitution is most worried about and least equipped to prevent.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 6: *Does this resist Babel-like temptations?* (Matt 6:24; Luke 12:16–21). The rich fool in Luke 12 built bigger barns. Victor builds more legal structures. The spirit is the same: using the letter of the law to protect accumulation the spirit of the law was meant to prevent.

**Verdict: Conditional.** The constitution names this risk and has the right provisions; the implementation must close the loopholes. Required fixes: (1) Independent auditor appointments must be governed by a rotating panel with conflict-of-interest exclusions — auditors cannot be appointed by the entity they audit. (2) "Purpose drift" must be defined: a project more than 24 months past its original milestone must undergo a published reset review or trigger partial reversion. (3) Worker-cooperative recognition must require minimum active membership, genuine profit-sharing, and regular governance participation — not just legal form.

## Group 8: Civic Corruption and Retaliation

### Vignette 13 — Priya

**Who she is.** Priya is 41 years old. She has an excellent Service Record: years of civic panel participation, verified community service, and a history of careful public reasoning. Six months ago, she discovered that the local Service Record administrator had been falsifying records — granting Service Record credit to politically connected individuals who had not performed the verified service. She reported this through the community alert pathway. The administrator, facing investigation, used their remaining administrative access to downgrade Priya's Service Record and flag several of her past contributions as "under review." Her eligibility for a public oversight role she had been pursuing was revoked pending resolution of the flags.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Article VI states that "any evidence of such use [of Service Record as patronage or corruption] must trigger audit and published correction within 60 days."
- Article VII's community alert pathway requires a published acknowledgment within 30 days and a formal review within 90 days if the alert meets a prima facie threshold.
- "Refusal to acknowledge or review is itself a reportable failure under this Article."
- The Federated Ombuds system is the appeal path for constitutional violations.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- The constitution protects the alert pathway and requires audit, but it does not explicitly protect the whistleblower from retaliation using the very system they reported. The administrator's action — downgrading the reporter's Service Record — is itself a reportable offense, but Priya must navigate the reporting system while her standing within it is under attack.
- The 60-day and 90-day windows for audit and review may be too slow to prevent the revocation of Priya's civic role. By the time the audit is published, the role has already gone to someone else.
- "Flags" on past contributions — placing them "under review" — appear to be an administrative action with no constitutional definition of how long a flag can remain, what evidence is required to place one, or who can remove it.
- The administrator's access was not immediately revoked upon complaint — they retained access long enough to act. This is a process failure, not a constitutional gap, but it indicates the system needs faster access revocation on credible complaints.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 4: *Does this honor truth, mercy, justice, forgiveness, and reconciliation?* And question 8: *Does this remain open to correction, repentance, accountability, and wise counsel?* (Prov 11:14). Priya did exactly what the system asks: she named a wrong through proper channels. She was punished for it. A system that cannot protect its own reporters from retaliation will quickly teach everyone not to report.

**Verdict: Conditional — urgent.** Required fixes: (1) Filing a community alert must trigger an automatic suspension of the respondent's administrative access to records involving the reporter — pending review, not pending conviction. (2) "Under review" flags must have a constitutional time limit (e.g., 45 days) and require independent authorization — not self-authorization by the flagging administrator. (3) Whistleblower protection must be explicitly named in the constitution: persons who file community alerts may not have their Service Record or Voice modified by any party who is the subject of the alert.

### Vignette 14 — Pastor Joel's Congregation

**Who they are.** Pastor Joel leads a congregation of 800 members in a mid-sized city. He is energetic, respected, and politically shrewd. He believes (sincerely) that his congregation's values should govern the city's priorities. He has organized a Voice coordination campaign: members are instructed each quarter on how to allocate their Voice to favor projects aligned with congregational values — a new community center on their land, zoning decisions favorable to their neighborhood, and a civic ethics panel dominated by congregation members.

**What the constitution guarantees.**
- Article VI: "No single contribution sector may dominate civic weighting." Voice is capped and balanced by category.
- Anti-capture weighting: Voice is "capped, fades quickly, balanced by category, and subject to conflict-of-interest rules."
- The integrity dashboards publish "capture" status under Article VII.
- Founding Legitimacy Dossier and Capture Dashboard Specification exist to detect exactly this pattern.

**What is underspecified or at risk.**
- "No single contribution sector may dominate" is a category-balance rule. Religious congregational coordination may not register as a "sector" in the system's classification — it may look like distributed individual Voice allocations from diverse people who happen to agree. The category balance rule cannot catch coordination that mimics diversity.
- The capture dashboard is designed to detect systemic capture at aggregate level; a congregation of 800 in a city of 20,000 — roughly 4% of the population — may not cross any aggregate threshold while still dominating neighborhood-level decisions.
- Coordination of Voice allocation is not prohibited by the constitution — political organizing is a protected activity. The constitution prohibits domination, not advocacy. The line between organized civic voice and captured civic weighting is definitional and contested.
- Conflict-of-interest rules for civic panels are mentioned but not defined. If congregation members sit on the panel that approves the congregation's community center project, this is a conflict of interest that the rule addresses — but the mechanism for detecting and enforcing it is not specified.

**The Christ-centered question.** Question 6: *Does this resist Babel-like temptations — pride, domination, coercive unity?* (Gen 11:1–9; Matt 20:25–28). This vignette is interesting precisely because Pastor Joel is not acting from obvious corruption — he genuinely believes he is serving his community. But Jesus warned that the desire to *do good through power* is the most seductive form of the Babel temptation. "You know that those who are regarded as rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them... Not so with you" (Mark 10:42–43).

**Verdict: Conditional.** The provisions exist; the detection mechanism is insufficient. Required fixes: (1) The conflict-of-interest rule must explicitly cover organizations, not just individuals: a congregation member may not participate in decisions that directly and materially benefit their congregation. (2) The capture dashboard must include neighborhood-level and project-level concentration metrics, not only city-wide sector aggregates. (3) A Voice coordination disclosure rule should be considered: organized campaigns to coordinate Voice across a membership should be disclosed, similar to political campaign disclosure, so the community can evaluate coordinated voice as such.

## Cross-Cutting Findings

## One-Year Pressure Stories

These scenarios test ordinary life over a full year. They are not meant to prove the system works. They are meant to show where fairness becomes strained when people live mixed, imperfect, normal lives.

Each story asks: what does the system protect, what does it still make people carry, and where could resentment or bureaucracy make the guarantee weaker in practice?

### Year Story 1 — Unstable Ordinary Worker

**Who they are.** Talia is 36. She works warehouse shifts and food-service weekends. She is not ideological. Most weeks she works, sees friends, streams shows, helps her nephew with homework, and ignores public meetings.

**The year.** In February her hours are cut. In April her mother has surgery, so Talia misses shifts and loses a supervisor's favor. In June she changes jobs. In August her rent share rises after a roommate leaves. By November she is working steadily again, but she has no cushion and is tired.

**What the system should do.**
- Essential Access keeps the job disruption from becoming hunger, untreated illness, or survival panic.
- Flow still matters: Talia feels the loss of extras, privacy, convenience, travel, gifts, and choice.
- Voice is available without making her perform civic virtue every month.
- Service Record should not treat family care and job instability as moral failure.

**Where fairness strains.**
- If every interruption requires proof, Talia spends her worst months doing paperwork.
- If active civic participants receive visibly more influence, she may feel governed by people with more free time.
- If Flow loss is too sharp, people will say the survival floor is real but the life above it is brittle.

**Fairness question.** Can the system protect normal instability without turning hardship into an application process?

**Likely verdict.** Conditional. The architecture is fair only if low-friction continuity exists for ordinary disruptions: job churn, caregiving, illness, rent changes, and short-term financial mistakes.

### Year Story 2 — Majority Lifestyle: Work, Play, Repeat

**Who they are.** Devon is 42. He works a steady municipal maintenance job, plays basketball twice a week, follows sports, takes one modest trip, and votes only when an issue feels direct. He is not a bad citizen. He is just busy and mostly content.

**The year.** Devon's routine is stable. He earns Flow, uses Essential Access for baseline needs, saves some, spends some, and rarely attends deliberation. In September a local infrastructure issue affects his commute. He uses Voice once, then disengages again.

**What the system should do.**
- Let Devon live a normal life without constant civic participation.
- Preserve meaningful reward for steady work.
- Keep basic needs separate from workplace pressure.
- Make civic tools usable in short bursts, not only for highly engaged people.

**Where fairness strains.**
- Organized minorities may dominate Voice if the majority is mostly passive.
- Devon may resent non-workers if public culture implies his work is morally optional but still depends on people like him.
- If demurrage is confusing, he may experience it as a penalty for ordinary prudence.

**Fairness question.** Does the system work when most people are neither heroic nor hostile, just occupied with daily life?

**Likely verdict.** Strong if the system assumes low participation as normal. Weak if it depends on unusually high civic attention.

### Year Story 3 — Caregiver Artist

**Who they are.** Lena is 51. She paints, sells a few pieces, and cares for her brother after a stroke. She does not hold a regular job. Her contribution is real but irregular, intimate, and hard to verify.

**The year.** In January she starts a small art series. In March her brother's condition worsens. In May she misses a community exhibition. In July she applies for recognition of caregiving contribution but finds the process intrusive. In October she sells two paintings and feels both proud and financially behind.

**What the system should do.**
- Keep her and her brother secure through Essential Access.
- Recognize caregiving without forcing surveillance of household life.
- Let art be meaningful without pretending every artist will earn Flow.
- Preserve Lena's dignity even when her contribution does not fit a workplace template.

**Where fairness strains.**
- Verification can become invasive.
- If caregiving is under-recognized, Lena looks inactive on paper.
- If caregiving is over-formalized, family life becomes administered.
- Other people may question why her art and care should count when their own work is tiring and measured.

**Fairness question.** Can the system honor unpaid care and creative work without creating fake metrics for private life?

**Likely verdict.** Conditional. This requires trusted attestation, light documentation, and a hard rule that Service Record measures eligibility for roles, not human worth.

### Year Story 4 — The Person Who Mostly Exists

**Who they are.** Aaron is 28. He does not work, volunteer, campaign, make art seriously, or care for dependents. He spends time online, walks around town, eats simply, socializes occasionally, and avoids responsibility. He is not dangerous. He is not secretly productive. He mostly exists.

**The year.** Nothing dramatic happens. He receives Essential Access. He has little Flow. He cannot afford many comforts. Friends drift away because they are busy. In August he briefly considers training, then stops. In December he is still alive, materially basic, and socially thin.

**What the system should do.**
- Protect his survival without humiliation.
- Avoid coercive activation rituals.
- Allow consequences above the floor: fewer choices, less comfort, less influence, less discretionary consumption.
- Resist the urge to invent a moral story where everyone eventually contributes.

**Where fairness strains.**
- Workers will notice him.
- Caregivers and high-burden professionals may resent him.
- Politicians may use people like Aaron as proof the system rewards idleness.
- Administrators may be tempted to make Essential Access conditional in subtle ways.

**Fairness question.** Is the system willing to protect bare human existence while preserving enough reward, honor, and relief for those carrying real burdens?

**Likely verdict.** This is the core test. If survival is truly unconditional, Aaron remains covered. The system must answer resentment honestly: equal survival does not mean equal lifestyle, authority, admiration, or Flow.

### Year Story 5 — High-Burden Skilled Worker

**Who they are.** Nia is 47. She is an emergency nurse. Her work is skilled, stressful, and socially necessary. She believes in the dignity floor but is exhausted by the number of people depending on systems she helps keep running.

**The year.** Winter illness strains the hospital. In spring, staffing improves briefly. In summer, a local shortage creates longer shifts. In October, Nia considers leaving for less stressful work. She sees non-workers secure in basic needs and feels conflicted: she does not want them harmed, but she wants the burden recognized.

**What the system should do.**
- Reward scarce and difficult work with Flow, rest protection, staffing safeguards, and social honor.
- Prevent professional skill from becoming political dominance.
- Protect Nia from being morally blackmailed into endless service.
- Keep the survival floor from depending on burnout.

**Where fairness strains.**
- If high-burden workers feel trapped by duty, the system recreates coercion through moral pressure.
- If they are over-rewarded with authority, a professional class forms.
- If they are under-rewarded, essential services decay.

**Fairness question.** How does the system retain excellence and sacrifice without letting excellence become rule over others?

**Likely verdict.** Conditional. The system needs capacity planning, rest floors, and compensation rules that distinguish reward from domination.

### Year Story 6 — Small Builder With Real Ambition

**Who they are.** Omar is 39. He repairs appliances and wants to expand into a cooperative repair shop. He is ambitious, practical, and not trying to dominate anyone. He wants more Flow because expansion requires tools, rent, inventory, and risk.

**The year.** In February he leases a small space. In April he hires one apprentice. In June cash flow is tight. In August he holds a larger balance for equipment purchases and worries about demurrage. In November the shop becomes viable.

**What the system should do.**
- Permit real accumulation for operating float, investment, and expansion.
- Keep markets alive enough that ambition has a place.
- Prevent business success from buying civic power.
- Distinguish productive capital from idle hoarding.

**Where fairness strains.**
- If rules treat all accumulation suspiciously, builders will feel punished.
- If exemptions are loose, wealthy actors will hide hoards as projects.
- If approval takes too long, small builders suffer more than sophisticated firms.

**Fairness question.** Can someone become materially better off by building useful capacity without converting wealth into power over others?

**Likely verdict.** Strong only if operating-float and project-capital rules are simple for small actors and strict for scalable abuse.

### Year Story 7 — Rule-Gamer

**Who they are.** Nolan is 33. He is not poor, desperate, or confused. He is clever and opportunistic. He tests boundaries: trading favors for Essential Access items, overstating hardship, creating fake contribution records, and routing Flow through friends.

**The year.** In March he finds a side market. In June he recruits others. In August a local provider notices patterns. In October enforcement begins. Nolan frames himself as persecuted and tries to turn the process into a political dispute.

**What the system should do.**
- Detect scalable abuse without treating every poor person as suspicious.
- Distinguish desperation, confusion, disability, and organized gaming.
- Prioritize enforcement against patterns that threaten the whole architecture.
- Provide appeal without letting appeal become endless delay.

**Where fairness strains.**
- Visible low-level misuse may get punished faster than sophisticated capture.
- Over-enforcement chills legitimate hardship claims.
- Under-enforcement damages worker trust and public legitimacy.

**Fairness question.** Are enforcement tools aimed at scalable harm rather than merely visible misuse?

**Likely verdict.** Conditional. Abuse control must be real, but enforcement should be proportional, appealable, and biased toward catching organized extraction over individual messiness.

### Year Story 8 — Dissenting Household

**Who they are.** Mara and Eli are married with two children. They dislike the system's moral and political assumptions. They use Essential Access because refusing it would hurt their children, but they do not participate in Voice and are considering moving to a non-participating jurisdiction.

**The year.** In January they withdraw from civic meetings. In May their children still receive school meals and healthcare through Essential Access. In September they explore exit. In December they remain because grandparents live nearby and relocation is expensive.

**What the system should do.**
- Protect dissenters without requiring gratitude.
- Keep children's survival access independent from parents' ideology.
- Make exit real, not retaliatory.
- Avoid treating non-participation as civic impurity.

**Where fairness strains.**
- If exit is formally allowed but practically unaffordable, freedom is partial.
- Local officials may treat dissenters as bad-faith actors.
- Children may become pressure points in ideological conflict.

**Fairness question.** Can someone reject the system and still receive dignity, due process, and practical freedom?

**Likely verdict.** Conditional. The exit protocol must protect dissenters in practice, not just in formal language.

### Annual Fairness Findings

These year stories suggest four fairness layers:

| Layer | Fairness rule | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| Survival | Protected because the person is human | Conditional access, humiliation, paperwork burden |
| Comfort and choice | Unequal through Flow, work, risk, creativity, and trade | Flattened incentives or brittle life above the floor |
| Public power | Not purchasable through wealth or popularity | Organized capture, professional class rule, wealthy routing |
| Trust and responsibility | Earned for role eligibility, never human worth | Service Record becomes rank, stigma, or retaliation tool |

The hardest fairness tension is not worker versus non-worker. It is burden versus unconditional dignity. A fair system must protect Aaron, who mostly exists, without exploiting Nia, who carries emergency work, or dismissing Lena, whose care is real but hard to measure.

- How much paperwork turns a right into a burden?
- Who helps people who cannot manage the system's forms, devices, deadlines, or appeals?
- Which parts of life remain none of the system's business?
- Can people be left alone without losing dignity or access?
- What makes high-burden workers stay without moral coercion?
- Can someone be poor in Flow but still socially respected?
- Can someone be wealthier than average without becoming politically stronger?
- How does the system distinguish fraud from confusion, trauma, disability, addiction, or bad luck?
- What happens when the majority is passive and organized minorities dominate Voice?
- What happens when local communities become clique-controlled or morally judgmental?
- How are false accusations, retaliation, and administrative flags handled quickly enough to matter?
- What happens when a person's needs are expensive, permanent, and unpopular?
- What does loneliness look like inside a materially secure system?
- What does year ten look like, after informal status groups have had time to harden?

### 1. The delivery gap is the biggest single risk

Multiple vignettes (Miriam, Ray, Amara, Elena, the Yoder Community, Margaret) show that the constitutional guarantee is strong but the delivery mechanism is underspecified. "Essential Access is unconditional" means nothing if the person cannot access it. The constitution needs a delivery sufficiency standard: for every person the guarantee covers, there must be a documented path by which they can actually receive it.

### 2. Protected Pause needs operational specification

The Protected Pause concept appears in Article VI but is not operationally defined. Who can trigger it? Who can verify it? How long does it last? What happens when the protected condition is permanent? Without answers, Protected Pause will be applied inconsistently and may be used as a bureaucratic tool against the people it is meant to protect.

### 3. Verification asymmetry punishes informal contributors

The verification burden falls on the contributor, not the system. Rosa, Father Emmanuel, the Kahananui family, and Daniel all contribute genuinely but cannot easily prove it through registered channels. The system's contribution-recognition mechanism must shift some of the verification burden to the system: regular community attestation processes, trusted-community-member verification, and sector-specific light-documentation paths.

### 4. Retaliation is the most urgent unclosed gap

Priya's case shows that the system's own tools can be turned against reporters and dissenters. This is not hypothetical — it is the standard pattern in every governance system that has faced insider corruption. Whistleblower protection must be named and operationalized in the constitution, not left to implementation.

### 5. The wealth-routing gap requires independent audit oversight

Victor's case shows that the protected-capital channels are necessary but insufficient. Without genuinely independent audit appointment — where auditors are not chosen by the entity they audit — the demurrage system's core anti-hoarding purpose can be circumvented by legal structuring. This is a solvable problem, but only if the implementation takes it seriously.

## Relationship to Christ-Centered Evaluation

These vignettes test the same eight questions as the Christ-Centered Evaluation, but from the bottom up — from the experience of specific people rather than the language of documents. Several findings require additions to `Christ_Centered_Evaluation.md`:

- **Babel-risk warning** for delivery gaps: a system that guarantees dignity floors unconditionally, then builds a delivery mechanism that excludes the most vulnerable, has reproduced the problem it names. The guarantee becomes a public relations statement rather than a lived reality.
- **Human dignity test** for verification asymmetry: requiring informal contributors to prove their contribution to a system that does not know them places the burden on persons who are already least equipped to carry it. This is the Pharisee pattern of Matt 23:4 — tying heavy loads, not lifting fingers.
- **Fruit test** for retaliation: if the community alert pathway produces retaliation against reporters, it will be abandoned within one generation. The good fruit of accountability requires that reporters be protected, not punished.
- **Revision proposal** across all vignettes: the constitution's governing sentence applies here — *The Humane Constitution must serve humanity under God; it must never become humanity's substitute for God.* For each gap named above, the revision is the same in spirit: find the person at the edge of the system's reach, and make sure the system reaches them — not because they have earned it, but because they are human.
