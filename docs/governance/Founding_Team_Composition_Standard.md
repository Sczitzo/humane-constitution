# Founding Team Composition Standard

**Status:** Active — Governance
**Scope:** Applies to the founding team of any Humane Constitution pilot town during pre-activation and the founder sunset period.

This document defines who may serve on the founding team, what disqualifies them, how the constitutional keyholder council is structured, and how founding team authority ends. The pilot town is a small built-from-scratch community (target: 1–5,000 residents). That means the founding team has enormous informal power during construction and early operation — power that does not appear in any org chart and is not checked by any election. This document exists to constrain that power structurally, not just rhetorically.

The failure mode this document guards against: a well-intentioned founding team that gradually becomes the permanent informal government of the place it built, answering to no one because it built the systems and knows all the people.

---

### FT-1 — Composition Floor

The founding team must include at minimum one member with professional competency in each of the following domains: public health, legal aid, social work or case management, construction or land development, and community finance or cooperative economics. Competency is demonstrated through at least three years of paid or credentialed work in the domain, not through self-assessment alone. The adversarial panel member verifies competency claims before Gate A clears.

At least one founding team member must have personally experienced housing insecurity, incarceration, or another category defined in VPCP-001 (Vulnerable Population Consent Protocol). This is not a diversity checkbox — it is a structural epistemic requirement. The founding team will make decisions that affect people whose lives most of the team has never lived. One person who has lived those lives does not fix the knowledge gap, but their absence guarantees the team will miss things that matter.

At least one founding team member must hold no financial interest in the pilot's success beyond their own labor compensation. This means: not an equity holder, not a donor who receives naming rights, not a future resident whose property value is tied to the pilot's performance. This person is the internal skeptic. Their job is not to slow things down — it is to notice when the team is rationalizing.

No more than 40% of founding team members may share a single professional background or a single institutional affiliation. This rule exists because founding teams assembled from one network tend to share one set of blind spots. A team of housing researchers, a team of tech entrepreneurs, and a team of faith community organizers will each miss different things. The 40% cap forces composition diversity that makes those blind spots harder to sustain.

### FT-2 — Disqualifying Conflicts

A person may not serve on the founding team if any of the following apply at the time of appointment or arise during their tenure:

**Vendor equity.** They hold an equity or profit interest in any vendor expected to receive more than $100,000 in contracts from the pilot. The threshold is cumulative across related entities. If a vendor relationship develops after appointment, the founding team member has 30 days to divest or resign. "Expected to receive" includes contracts not yet signed if negotiations are underway.

**Family concentration.** They have a first- or second-degree family relationship (parent, child, sibling, grandparent, grandchild, aunt, uncle, niece, nephew, or equivalent by marriage) with another founding team member, unless the relationship is disclosed in writing and reviewed by a civil-society reviewer unaffiliated with the pilot. Disclosure does not automatically clear the conflict — the reviewer may require structural separation of responsibilities. This rule exists because family networks create informal authority structures that are nearly impossible to challenge from outside.

**Regulatory employment.** They are currently employed by a government agency with regulatory authority over the pilot site — zoning, environmental permitting, health licensing, or equivalent. The conflict here is not corruption; it is dual accountability that neither role can honor well.

**Financial crimes or fiduciary breaches.** They have a history of financial crimes or documented fiduciary breaches within the last 10 years. Self-disclosure is required at application. An independent background check is also required. A finding does not automatically disqualify — the review process examines nature, severity, and remediation. But non-disclosure of a known history is automatic disqualification.

### FT-3 — Keyholder Seat Allocation (9 Seats)

The constitutional keyholder council for the pilot operates on a 9-of-9 structure with a 7-of-9 threshold for Tier 1 constitutional changes. Seats are allocated as follows.

No founding team member may permanently occupy a keyholder seat after the founder sunset date (defined in FS-1). Founding team members may hold seats temporarily during the pre-activation phase only. All seats must transition to nominated or elected holders before the first resident cohort reaches 90 days of residency.

**Seat 1–2: Resident Elected.** Nominated and elected by residents with 90 or more days of continuous residency. The founding team has no role in the nomination or election process beyond administering the logistics if no other administrator is available — and in that case, a civil-society reviewer must observe the process. These seats represent the community's direct voice on constitutional matters.

**Seat 3–4: External Civil Society.** Nominated by organizations with no financial interest in the pilot. Nominees must not be former founding team members. "External" means the nominating organization operates independently of the pilot's governance and has no contractual relationship with its funders. These seats are designed to represent interests the community itself may not yet have organized around.

**Seat 5: Adversarial Panel Representative.** Nominated by the adversarial panel defined in the Founding Legitimacy Dossier. This seat exists to give the pilot's most persistent external critics a voice in its constitutional decisions. The adversarial panel representative is not required to support the pilot — they are required to apply their critical lens honestly.

**Seat 6: Legal/Constitutional.** Nominated by a legal aid or public defender organization in the region. The nominating organization must have a primary mission of serving low-income clients, not commercial legal practice. This seat ensures the council includes someone whose professional formation is built around rights protection rather than institutional administration.

**Seat 7: Public Health and Social Services.** Nominated by a public health body or social services organization in the region. The nominating organization must not be a funder or contractor of the pilot.

**Seat 8: Financial/Operations.** Nominated by a worker-owned cooperative or union organization. This seat ensures the council includes someone with accountability structures different from employer-employee and investor-portfolio relationships.

**Seat 9: Resident Lottery.** Selected by random lottery from residents with 18 or more months of continuous residency. The lottery is administered publicly. No founding team member may influence the pool or the selection process. This seat exists because elections favor residents who are already visible and socially connected; the lottery surfaces residents who may never be elected but have lived the community's realities longer than most.

---

### FT-4 — Nomination Process for Seats 3–8

Nominating organizations must be identified and approached before the Founding Legitimacy Dossier's Gate A clears. This is not a post-activation task. If the founding team has not identified candidate nominating organizations by Gate A, Gate A does not clear.

Nominating organizations may not be selected by the founding team alone. Each selection must be approved by the adversarial panel member. This approval is not a rubber stamp — the adversarial panel member may reject an organization if it has undisclosed ties to the pilot's funders, if its leadership includes former founding team members, or if its independence cannot be verified.

Each nominating organization must confirm in writing that it has no financial relationship with the pilot or its funders — no grants received, no contracts pending, no gifts accepted, no board membership overlap. This confirmation is public.

For Seats 3–8, "civil society" uses the independence logic in the [Founding Consent and Civil-Society Review Evidence Test Package](./Founding_Consent_Civil_Society_Review_Evidence_Test_Package.md): no financial or governance dependence on founders, funders, vendors, or pilot administration; disclosed conflicts; affected-community accountability where applicable; and a record showing capacity to dissent. This does not mean every keyholder nominating body must be a Level 7 founding reviewer. It means the founding team may not use weaker "outside organization" language to route keyholder nominations through friendly or dependent bodies.

If a nominating organization cannot be identified for any seat within 60 days of Gate A, that seat defaults to a resident election using the same 90-day residency requirement as Seats 1–2. This default is not a failure mode — it is a safety valve. A pilot with strong resident participation may prefer resident elections over external nominations. What is not acceptable is a vacant seat.

### KH-1 — Keyholder Independence Test

Before any keyholder vote on a Tier 1 matter, the adversarial panel member must run an independence check. The check asks: are any two keyholders connected through shared employer, shared funder, family relationship, or documented political coordination within the past 24 months?

If yes, the affected keyholder must recuse from the vote, or the vote is delayed 30 days for a public disclosure period. During the disclosure period, the nature of the connection is published and residents may comment. After 30 days, the vote proceeds with the affected keyholder recusing or, if the keyholder disputes the independence finding, with a ruling from the adversarial panel member that is final.

The independence test is not about suspicion of bad faith. Keyholders from similar networks may share values without coordinating. But Tier 1 constitutional decisions require that the council not function as a single bloc. The test is structural, not accusatory.

---

### KH-2 — Keyholder Rotation

Each keyholder seat has a maximum 4-year term. Terms are staggered at initial formation so that no more than three seats turn over in any 18-month period.

Seats 1–2 (Resident Elected) and Seat 9 (Resident Lottery) are renewable. Residents elect or re-draw from the eligible pool at the end of each term. There is no term limit on these seats beyond the structural checks above.

Seats 3–8 (Nominated) rotate to new nominating organizations after each term. The same individual may not be renominated by a new organization as a mechanism for extending their tenure — if a person has served one full term in any nominated seat, they are ineligible for any other nominated seat for 4 years following the end of their term.

---

### KH-3 — Keyholder Disclosure

All keyholders are disclosed publicly: their name, their nominating organization or selection method, any conflicts of interest they disclosed at the time of appointment, and any conflicts that arose and were reviewed during their tenure. This disclosure is maintained in the pilot's public governance record and updated within 14 days of any change.

---

### KH-4 — Disqualifying Conflicts During Tenure

A keyholder who develops a disqualifying conflict — defined by the same standards as FT-2 with adjustments for the keyholder role — must recuse from affected votes within 7 days of discovering the conflict and must either resolve the conflict or vacate the seat within 14 days. If they do not vacate, the adversarial panel member initiates a removal review. Removal requires a 6-of-9 keyholder vote, excluding the affected keyholder.

---

### KH-5 — Meeting Records

Keyholder meeting records — including attendance, agenda items, votes cast, and dissenting statements — are public within 7 days of each meeting. Summary publication is not sufficient; the full record is published. Closed-session discussions are permitted only for matters involving resident privacy, ongoing legal proceedings, or security vulnerabilities. The existence of a closed session and its general subject must still be disclosed within 7 days.

### FS-1 — Founder Sunset Date

The founder sunset date is the earlier of: (a) 90 days after Cohort 1 reaches full occupancy as defined in the pilot's occupancy agreement, or (b) 24 months after the first permanent resident arrives. Whichever date comes first is the sunset date. The date is calculated publicly and announced no later than 30 days before it arrives.

---

### FS-2 — Loss of Constitutional Authority

On the sunset date, all founding team members lose any constitutional authority, appointment power, or keyholder position they hold by virtue of being founders. This loss is automatic and does not require a vote. If a founding team member is also a resident-elected or lottery-selected keyholder at the sunset date, they retain that seat — because they hold it as a resident, not as a founder. Any seat they hold by founding team appointment is vacated on the sunset date and filled through the standard nomination or election process.

---

### FS-3 — Post-Sunset Presence

Founding team members may remain in the community after the sunset date as residents, employees of community organizations, or contractors to the pilot's governance bodies. What they may not do is hold any governance position — elected, appointed, or advisory — for 24 months following the sunset date, unless they are elected by current residents through a standard election with no preferential treatment based on their founding role.

The 24-month restriction applies to formal and informal governance roles. If a founding team member is functioning as a de facto decision-maker — consulted routinely before major decisions, present in keyholder meetings as an advisor, directing staff who implement governance decisions — that is a governance role and triggers the restriction regardless of what it is called.

---

### FS-4 — Sunset Report

Each founding team member must produce or co-produce a public sunset report before the sunset date. The report documents: what the founding team built; what they attempted and failed; what decisions they made that residents later challenged or reversed; what informal authority the founding team member currently holds and is actively releasing on the sunset date; and what they would do differently.

The sunset report is not a success narrative. A sunset report that contains no failures, no mistakes, and no honest accounting of informal authority is rejected by the adversarial panel member and must be revised before the sunset date.

---

### FS-5 — Adversarial Panel Review of Sunset Report

The sunset report must be submitted to the adversarial panel member no later than 45 days before the sunset date. The adversarial panel member has 30 days to respond with findings. If the report is rejected, the founding team member has 15 days to revise and resubmit. If the revised report is also rejected, the sunset proceeds but the panel member's findings are published alongside the report.

---

### FS-6 — Post-Sunset Governance Re-entry

Any founding team member who attempts to re-enter governance before the 24-month post-sunset period ends — through any formal or informal channel — triggers an automatic Perpetual Humility Review as defined below. The review is triggered by the act of re-entry, not by its intent. Good intentions do not pause the review.

---

### FS-7 — Financial Benefit from Authorship

Founding team members who profit financially from authorship of the pilot's narrative — books, speaking engagements, consulting contracts that trade on their founding role, documentary participation fees, and equivalent — redirect 50% of that compensation to the communal fund for 5 years following the sunset date. This obligation is disclosed to all founding team members before the pilot begins and acknowledged in writing as a condition of joining the founding team.

The rationale: the founding team will be able to tell the story of this place for the rest of their lives, in ways residents cannot. Residents do not receive speaking fees for living through what the founding team built. The 50% redirect is not punishment — it is a recognition that narrative profit from this community belongs partly to the community.

### CS-1 — Resident Notice for Founding Period Decisions

During the founding period (pre-sunset), founding team decisions that affect residents require advance notice to residents of at least 7 days and a public comment period of at least 5 days. Notice must be delivered through channels accessible to all residents — not only those who attend founding team meetings or follow digital communication.

Decisions that are not prospective — emergency responses to safety risks, time-sensitive legal filings, and equivalent — are exempt from the advance notice requirement but must be disclosed within 48 hours of being made, with an explanation of why advance notice was not possible.

---

### CS-2 — Resident Opposition Review

Any founding team decision opposed by more than 30% of current residents — measured by a formal expression of opposition through a process defined in advance, not by the founding team's informal read of the room — must be reviewed by the adversarial panel member before taking effect. The review is not a veto. The panel member may clear the decision, require modifications, or recommend a resident vote. Their recommendation is published. If the founding team proceeds against a recommendation to halt or modify, that decision is logged as a contested action in the public governance record.

---

### CS-3 — Founding Team Compensation

Founding team compensation is public. Individual compensation is disclosed in the monthly status report (CS-4). No founding team member's total compensation — including salary, equity equivalents, housing subsidies tied to the role, and other benefits — may exceed 150% of the median resident compensation for the same period. If the pilot has not yet established resident compensation benchmarks, the founding team uses regional median income as the baseline.

This rule exists because founding teams that pay themselves well above what residents earn gradually lose the capacity to understand what scarcity feels like in the community they govern. That is not a moral claim — it is an empirical one.

---

### CS-4 — Monthly Status Report

The founding team publishes a monthly status report covering: finances (income, expenditures, outstanding obligations, and variance from budget); construction and infrastructure progress; resident wellbeing indicators as defined in the Vulnerable Population Consent Protocol; and consent health (whether the community's ongoing consent processes are functioning, what has been challenged, and what has been resolved). The report is public and written in plain language accessible to residents without professional backgrounds in finance or governance.

---

### CS-5 — Founding Team Removal

The founding team may be removed in whole or in part by a 60% vote of current residents at any time during the founding period. The vote is initiated by a petition signed by 25% of residents. The petition triggers a 14-day deliberation period during which the founding team may respond publicly. After 14 days, the vote is held. If 60% of residents vote to remove, removal takes effect within 30 days, during which time the adversarial panel member oversees a transition to interim governance.

---

### Perpetual Humility Review

The Perpetual Humility Review is triggered by FS-6 (founding team member attempts governance re-entry before the 24-month post-sunset restriction ends) or by a resident petition signed by 20% of current residents at any time post-sunset.

The review is conducted by the adversarial panel member. It begins with the founding team reading aloud — collectively, or individually if collective reading is not possible — the Humility Declaration:

> "We, the founding team, built what we could. We did not build what was needed. We brought our best intentions and our worst blind spots. We made decisions that affected people whose lives we could not fully understand. We didn't figure out shit. That is why every check in this document exists."

This declaration is not a ritual of humiliation. It is a structural reset — a requirement that the people who built this place acknowledge, in front of the people who live in it, that building something does not make you its authority.

After the declaration, the review evaluates three questions:

**(a) Informal authority re-entry.** Does any founding team member currently hold informal authority that has re-entered governance in practice — through advisory roles, staff relationships, financial leverage, or social influence that functions as decision-making power?

**(b) Resident capacity to challenge.** Are there residents who feel unable to challenge founding team members, contest founding team decisions, or raise grievances without social or economic cost? The adversarial panel member interviews a sample of residents, including recent arrivals, to assess this.

**(c) Corrective actions required.** Based on findings from (a) and (b), what structural changes — in roles, relationships, compensation, or governance design — are required? The panel member's corrective action recommendations are binding unless overturned by a 7-of-9 keyholder vote.

The Perpetual Humility Review recurs every 3 years post-sunset for as long as any founding team member lives in or works with the community in any capacity. The recurrence is not contingent on problems being found. It is contingent on founding team members remaining present. The founding team's presence is the reason for the review.
