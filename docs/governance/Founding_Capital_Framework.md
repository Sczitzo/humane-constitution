# Founding Capital Framework

**Status:** Reference — Founding Phase

**Scope:** Defines how the pilot town (1–5,000 people, built from scratch) raises, holds, and disburses its founding capital. Applies from first fundraising contact through Year 5 post-activation.

**Constitutional instruments covered:** Flow (market money), Essential Access (survival floor), Voice (civic weight), Service Record (contribution history), Shared Storehouse (emergency rationing).

---

### FC-1 — Phase Capital Targets

- Phase 1 (land acquisition + infrastructure design): $15–22M
- Phase 2 (core infrastructure + first residential units): $35–55M
- Phase 3 (full buildout to 1,000+ residents): $60–90M

These ranges assume a US rural/peri-urban site with favorable land cost. Actual targets must be set by an independent financial review before Gate A (Founding Legitimacy Dossier). The financial review is conducted by a firm with no existing relationship to the founding team or any committed funder.

---

### FC-2 — Concentration Limits

No single funder — individual, foundation, government agency, or corporation — may contribute more than 20% of total committed capital across all phases.

No single funder may contribute more than 30% of capital in any single phase.

These limits are calculated on committed capital at the time of each funding agreement, not at financial close.

If a funder's share would exceed the limit because a subsequent funder withdraws, the Capital Steward must identify replacement funding or reduce scope before drawing on the oversized funder's capital. The Capital Steward does not disburse against a concentration-limit breach.

---

### FC-3 — Capital Steward

All founding capital is held by an independent Capital Steward entity — a nonprofit fiduciary or trust company with no financial interest in the pilot's success or failure.

The Capital Steward is not appointed by the founding team. It is selected by a process approved by the adversarial panel member named in the Founding Legitimacy Dossier.

The Capital Steward disburses capital only against approved construction milestones and operational budgets reviewed by the keyholder council. Disbursement requests not accompanied by a milestone completion record are declined.

The Capital Steward publishes monthly disbursement reports as publicly accessible documents. Reports include: amounts disbursed, milestone tied to each disbursement, remaining phase capital, and contingency reserve balance.

---

### FC-4 — Constitutional Primacy Clause

Every funding agreement must contain language substantially as follows:

> "This agreement is subordinate to the Humane Constitution and its implementing documents as amended from time to time by the governing keyholder council. In any conflict between this agreement and the Constitution, the Constitution governs. Funder's contractual rights do not include the right to override, suspend, or modify constitutional protections for residents."

This clause is non-negotiable. If a funder will not sign an agreement containing this clause, the funding is declined. The Capital Steward does not accept funds from an agreement that omits or qualifies this clause.

---

### FC-5 — Government Funding Walk-Away Rule

If a government agency — federal, state, county, or municipal — conditions its funding on the right to:

- Access resident data without individual consent
- Override constitutional protections for any resident population
- Require the pilot to cooperate with immigration enforcement
- Modify the Essential Access floor or exit rights

...then the founding team must decline the funding. The refusal and its basis are documented and published within 30 days of the refusal.

A government agency that will not sign a non-interference acknowledgment does not receive a funding agreement. The non-interference acknowledgment states that the agency's funding relationship does not alter the pilot's obligations to residents under the Humane Constitution.

This rule applies regardless of funding amount. There is no waiver process.

---

### FC-6 — Contingency Reserve

A contingency reserve equal to 25% of each phase's capital must be held in escrow before construction begins for that phase.

The reserve may only be drawn with joint approval of the Capital Steward and the keyholder council. A single-party draw request is not executed.

If the reserve drops below 15% of phase capital, construction in that phase pauses until the reserve is restored to 25%. The pause applies to all new disbursements; existing committed subcontracts are not disrupted mid-delivery.

---

### FC-7 — Pre-Activation Flow Issuance

Construction workers and other paid contributors during the founding period are paid in both external currency (for external expenses) and in Flow (for internal ledger credit).

The Flow portion of construction payroll qualifies as productive commitment under the constitutional Flow issuance model, shortening the path to Flow self-sufficiency.

The Flow exchange rate for construction payroll is set by the keyholder council, not the founding team. The rate is published before any construction payroll is issued. Mid-phase rate changes require keyholder council approval and are applied prospectively, not retroactively.

---

### FC-8 — Community Land Trust Structure

The pilot site is held in a Community Land Trust (CLT) structure: a nonprofit trust holds the land in perpetuity; residents hold ground leases.

The CLT governing board must include resident representatives at a minimum of 30% of board seats from the first cohort forward. No funder holds a CLT board seat.

The CLT articles of incorporation require that the land cannot be sold, developed contrary to the constitutional purpose, or encumbered by debt without a 60% resident vote and keyholder council approval.

No funder receives naming rights, equity, or reversionary interest in the land. Any agreement purporting to grant such interests is void and not executed by the Capital Steward.

---

### FC-9 — Naming and Influence Prohibitions

No facility, street, neighborhood, or community institution may be named after a funder without a community vote conducted under the pilot's Voice instrument.

No funder may hold a seat on the keyholder council, the founding team, or the Capital Steward board.

No funder may designate a specific resident or employee of the pilot as a condition of funding.

Violation of any of these prohibitions triggers FC-2 recalculation: the violating funder's contribution is recalculated at 0% for future phases, and the founding team must identify replacement capital before the next phase begins.

---

### FC-10 — Earned Income and Internal Economy

The pilot must develop a plan for earned income sufficient to cover ongoing operational costs by Year 5 post-activation.

Earned income sources include: the internal Flow economy, local agricultural and manufacturing production, service contracts with external parties, and housing fees structured as ground-lease payments to the CLT.

The Year 5 earned income plan is published and reviewed by the keyholder council before Gate A. The plan includes: projected revenue by source, assumptions for each projection, and the conditions under which each source fails.

---

### FC-11 — Financial Transparency

Full financial statements — income, expenses, capital accounts, contingency reserve balance — are published quarterly. Statements are published within 45 days of each quarter's end.

All funding agreements are public documents. Personal data of individual donors (home address, tax ID) may be redacted; funding amount, funder name, and constitutional primacy clause are not redacted.

Any funder conversation in which constitutional primacy (FC-4) or the walk-away rule (FC-5) was invoked is documented and published within 30 days of that conversation. Documentation includes: the date, the funder, the condition offered, and the founding team's response.

---

### FC-12 — Failure and Wind-Down Reserve

Before Cohort 1 arrives, a wind-down reserve equal to 6 months of Essential Access delivery for the expected first cohort must be pre-funded and held by the Capital Steward.

This reserve is drawn only in the event of pilot wind-down. It is used exclusively for resident transition support: temporary housing bridge, healthcare continuity, and Service Record export to a format usable outside the pilot.

The wind-down reserve is a separate account from the contingency reserve (FC-6). Draws against one account do not affect the other. The wind-down reserve is not available for construction cost overruns.

---

## Honest Limitations

This framework does not fully solve three problems:

**1. Informal funder influence not captured in funding agreements.** Concentration limits and naming prohibitions apply to formal contractual relationships. A funder with 18% of capital can still cultivate informal relationships with founding team members, provide in-kind services that create dependency, or set conditions in side communications that never appear in the agreement. The framework has no mechanism for detecting or disciplining influence that operates outside documented channels.

**2. Concentration limit evasion through affiliated entities.** FC-2 calculates concentration on committed capital per named funder. A funder with affiliated foundations, family offices, or corporate subsidiaries can route contributions through multiple entities to stay below the per-funder threshold while controlling a larger share of total capital. This is now routed to the Anti-Rent Legal Wrapper Evidence Test Package and Hardening Queue rank 4. Before any construction financing draw, affiliated entities must be aggregated, the Capital Steward must review beneficial-control and side-letter disclosures, and unresolved affiliation/control questions block the draw. The framework still needs field-tested enforcement evidence before claiming this route is closed.

**3. Government regulatory authority that operates independently of the funding relationship.** FC-5 blocks government funders who condition funding on constitutional violations. It does not block a government agency from exercising regulatory authority — zoning, health, building code, tax — in ways that constrain the pilot's constitutional choices without triggering the funding walk-away rule. A state agency could decline to fund the pilot while simultaneously using licensing authority to require resident data disclosure or to restrict Essential Access eligibility. The framework has no answer to regulatory coercion that arrives through channels other than the funding agreement.
