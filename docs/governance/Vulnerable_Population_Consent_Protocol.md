# Vulnerable Population Consent Protocol (VPCP)

**Status: Active — Governance**

**Purpose:** Define the consent standards that govern how the pilot town recruits residents who are in a position of material vulnerability at the time of first contact. These standards are stricter than the general consent process and cannot be waived by the founding team or by the prospective resident.

---

### VPCP-001 — Scope and Applicability

This protocol applies to any prospective resident who, at the time of first contact, is in one or more of the following categories:

- Experiencing homelessness or housing insecurity (including couch-surfing, shelter stays, or imminent eviction)
- Unemployed for 90 or more consecutive days
- Released from incarceration within the prior 24 months
- Fleeing domestic violence or human trafficking
- Aging out of foster care (age 18–25)
- In active addiction recovery (including participation in treatment programs, medication-assisted treatment, or peer recovery support)
- Receiving means-tested public benefits as primary income support (SNAP, SSI, SSDI, TANF, Medicaid, Section 8/HCV, or state equivalents)

Where a prospective resident meets multiple categories, the most protective provision applicable to each relevant category governs. The founding team may not argue that one category cancels out another.

A prospective resident outside VPCP-001 scope who enters the pilot and later discloses a qualifying condition must be offered VPCP protections retroactively from the point of disclosure.

---

### VPCP-002 — Pre-Offer Stability Requirement

Before any formal offer of residency is made to a person in VPCP-001 scope, all three of the following must be complete and delivered to the prospective resident in writing:

**Unconditional transitional support pre-arrangement.** Ninety days of transitional support — covering housing, food, and healthcare continuity — must be pre-arranged and funded regardless of whether the person joins the pilot. This support is not contingent on continued interest in joining, on good behavior, or on any other condition. The founding team must document the funding source, the service provider, and the person's confirmed access before making the offer.

**Benefits impact assessment.** A written analysis must be produced showing which public benefits the person currently receives, which they may lose or risk upon joining the pilot (with timeline and estimated dollar amounts), and what steps, if any, can mitigate that loss. This document must be written in plain language (6th-grade reading level or below) and translated into the person's primary language if it differs from English.

**Alternatives presentation.** At least three realistic alternative pathways to stable housing or support must be identified and presented in writing. These must be actual available options — not theoretical programs — with contact information and the founding team's honest assessment of accessibility. The purpose is to demonstrate that the person is not being presented with a false binary between joining the pilot and having nothing.

The founding team must not make the offer until all three deliverables are complete and confirmed received by the prospective resident.

---

### VPCP-003 — Independent Consent Advocate (ICA)

**Appointment.** The ICA is not appointed by the founding team. ICA staffers are nominated by a civil-society panel composed of organizations with no financial interest in the pilot (examples: legal aid organizations, housing nonprofits, formerly incarcerated persons' advocacy groups, domestic violence service providers). The founding team funds the ICA but has no authority over hiring, assignment, or findings.

**Staffing requirement.** At least 50% of ICA capacity serving VPCP-scope residents must be staffed by trained peer specialists — people with lived experience of homelessness, incarceration, addiction recovery, domestic violence, foster care aging-out, or the specific vulnerability category relevant to the resident. Peer specialist training must meet a recognized standard (e.g., state certification or equivalent).

**ICA role.** The ICA meets with the prospective resident privately, without founding team members present, before and during the cooling-off period. The ICA explains the offer, answers questions, identifies risks the resident may not have considered, and gives the resident a direct channel to raise concerns. The ICA does not advocate for the pilot; they advocate for the prospective resident's informed autonomy.

**Adverse findings.** The ICA has authority to issue an adverse finding if it determines that consent conditions are not being met for a category of prospective residents. An adverse finding immediately pauses all recruitment outreach targeting that VPCP-001 category until a 14-day civil-society response period completes. The founding team must respond in writing to any adverse finding within 14 days. If the founding team disagrees with the finding, the dispute is referred to the adversarial panel member on the keyholder council, whose determination is binding. ICA findings, founding team responses, and keyholder council determinations are public documents.

---

### VPCP-004 — Cooling-Off Period

After all VPCP-002 materials have been delivered and the ICA has completed at least one session with the prospective resident, a mandatory 30-day cooling-off period begins.

During the cooling-off period:

- No commitment documents may be presented, signed, or executed.
- The founding team may not initiate contact with the prospective resident. The founding team may respond to questions the resident initiates, and must do so promptly, but may not use responses as an opportunity to promote the offer.
- The prospective resident may withdraw interest at any point during the cooling-off period at no cost, penalty, or loss of the VPCP-002 transitional support.

After the cooling-off period ends, the prospective resident may choose to proceed, extend their consideration, or decline. Declining at any point forfeits no transitional support and creates no obligation.

---

### VPCP-005 — Informed Understanding Verification

Before the prospective resident signs any commitment document, the founding team must verify comprehension using the teach-back method: the resident explains back in their own words what they are agreeing to, what rights they retain, what they are giving up, what the pilot can change and cannot change about their situation, and how they can exit.

Plain-language materials (6th-grade reading level or below) must be provided in the resident's primary language. If professional translation is needed, the founding team pays for it.

Understanding verification is not a gatekeeping test. It is the founding team's obligation to ensure comprehension, not the resident's obligation to pass a test. If a resident cannot demonstrate understanding after reasonable support — additional explanation, different materials, interpreter assistance, more time — the offer is suspended until understanding is achievable. The offer is not declined because of comprehension difficulty; it is held open.

The ICA must be present for or promptly informed of the teach-back session. If the ICA has concerns about the quality of comprehension, it may issue a finding under VPCP-003.

---

### VPCP-006 — Exit Rights (Non-Waivable)

Regardless of what the resident agrees to in any commitment document, the following rights cannot be waived by any party:

- **Housing continuity.** Sixty days of continued access to their current housing unit after declaring exit intent.
- **Healthcare continuity.** Ninety days of continued access to healthcare services after declaring exit intent.
- **Essential Access continuity.** The resident's Survival Floor / Essential Access tier continues for 90 days post-exit, regardless of Tier 1 standing at the time of exit.
- **No financial penalty.** No debt, clawback, or financial penalty for exiting. Residency investments made by the community on behalf of a resident — infrastructure improvements, skill development expenditures, service credits — belong to the community, not the exiting resident, and cannot be converted into exit obligations.
- **Service Record export.** The resident's full Service Record is exported in a portable, machine-readable format within 7 days of exit request. The resident owns their record.
- **Return-to-prior-status support.** If the resident was in VPCP-001 scope upon entry, they are entitled to a 90-day return-to-prior-status support package funded by the pilot. This package assists re-connection with public benefits, housing waitlists, and community support networks that may have been disrupted by participation in the pilot.

These rights are incorporated by reference into every resident agreement. Any agreement term that purports to waive or limit them is void.

---

### VPCP-007 — Ongoing Consent

**Annual re-affirmation.** Each VPCP-scope resident is offered an ICA session annually. The session reviews whether the resident's current situation, understanding of the system, and consent to continued participation remain current. The session is the resident's, not the founding team's — the resident sets the agenda. Declining the annual session is not a problem; the offer must be made.

**Change-triggered review.** Any material change to pilot governance, living conditions, community rules, or Essential Access terms triggers a mandatory ICA session for all VPCP-scope residents within 30 days of the change taking effect. "Material change" is defined broadly: when in doubt, it triggers. The ICA determines whether a change is material; the founding team does not.

**Annual consent health report.** The ICA publishes an annual public report covering: number of VPCP-scope residents enrolled and active, adverse findings issued and their resolution, exit rates by VPCP-001 category, resident-initiated concerns and how they were addressed, and ICA staffing compliance with the peer-specialist requirement. The founding team may not edit or delay this report.

---

### VPCP-008 — Pre-Recruitment Prerequisites

No recruitment outreach targeting VPCP-001 scope populations may begin until all four of the following conditions are met and documented:

- **VPCP-002 support infrastructure is operational.** Transitional support funding and service agreements are in place and verified, not merely planned. Documentation must include executed contracts with service providers and confirmed funding in an escrow or restricted account.
- **ICA is staffed and ready.** At least two ICA staffers are trained, available, and confirmed — including at least one peer specialist. Neither may have been appointed by the founding team.
- **Exit support fund is pre-capitalized.** The fund covers a minimum of 90 days of VPCP-006 benefits (housing, healthcare, Essential Access continuity, and return-to-prior-status support) for the full expected first-cohort size. The fund must be held in a restricted account and cannot be used for other purposes without civil-society panel approval.
- **Founding Legitimacy Dossier consent evidence is at PRODUCED status for VPCP-scope populations.** The general consent evidence artifact must be at PRODUCED status, and the VPCP adds an additional vulnerable-population consent evidence artifact that must separately reach PRODUCED status. This artifact documents that all VPCP-002 through VPCP-007 mechanisms are operational and that the civil-society panel has reviewed and affirmed the ICA appointment process.

---

### Residual Risk and Honest Limitations

This protocol does not fully solve every consent problem that arises when vulnerable people join an intentional community. Four limitations deserve explicit acknowledgment:

**Social pressure from existing residents.** Once inside the community, a new resident encounters people who have made the pilot their home and who have strong social and reputational stakes in its success. That pressure is real and this protocol has no mechanism for it. A resident who wants to leave may face soft discouragement from peers who are not founding team members and who act in good faith but still make exit harder.

**Informal status hierarchies once living inside the community.** The pilot's contribution-tracking system creates visible differences in standing. Even with a Survival Floor guarantee, residents who came in with lower service records may experience informal deference to longer-tenured or higher-contributing residents. These hierarchies can operate on vulnerable new arrivals in ways that constrain voice and exit even when formal rights exist.

**Gratitude-debt.** Residents who arrived from homelessness, incarceration, or other acute distress may feel profound gratitude to the community that gave them stability. Gratitude is not coercion, but it creates a psychological bind — voicing dissent or leaving can feel like betrayal of people who helped you. This protocol cannot dissolve that bind. The best it can do is name it explicitly and make the ICA available as a private channel for processing it.

**Exit rights that exist on paper but are hard to use.** Sixty days of continued housing and 90 days of healthcare are meaningful, but a resident who has disengaged from outside support networks during their time in the pilot may face a steep re-entry cliff even with full use of VPCP-006 rights. The return-to-prior-status package is a partial response, but reconnecting to public benefits, housing waitlists, and community ties takes longer than 90 days for many people. The protocol names this gap honestly rather than pretending the exit rights make leaving easy.
