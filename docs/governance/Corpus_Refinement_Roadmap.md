# Corpus Refinement Roadmap

The goal is not to make the project sound bigger. The goal is to make the moral center easier to feel, the mechanisms easier to inspect, and the evidence gaps harder to hide.

## Desired Reader Experience

A first-time reader should move through four stages:

1. **Human stakes:** why survival, markets, and public power must not collapse into one tool.
2. **Moral clarity:** what the project is trying to protect, and what it refuses to buy with efficiency.
3. **Mechanism:** how Flow, Essential Access, Voice, Service Record, and Shared Storehouse are separated.
4. **Accountability:** how the design can fail, what evidence is missing, and what would downgrade its claims.

The first emotional signal should be: *this is serious about human dignity and honest about risk.*

## Refinement Principles

- Lead with people before architecture.
- Make every major claim status-visible: moral commitment, designed mechanism, active-unproven control, partly tested, needs evidence, or unresolved prerequisite.
- Keep beauty disciplined. Awe should come from clarity, restraint, and moral seriousness.
- Invite skepticism as a form of stewardship.
- Do not imply deployment readiness.
- Do not imply divine endorsement. Christ-centered review judges the project; it does not consecrate it.
- Preserve technical depth, but stop making every curious reader climb the whole audit stack first.

## Moral Posture

The public voice should be righteous without becoming self-righteous.

Righteous means the corpus should plainly defend the vulnerable, tell the truth about power, resist domination, protect dissent, and refuse to measure human worth. It should not sound triumphant, inevitable, or spiritually authorized.

Use this posture:

> This is a human attempt to design rules that serve people rather than dominate them. It must be judged by whether it protects the vulnerable, preserves dignity, tells the truth about failure, and remains open to correction.

Do not use language that implies the system saves, sanctifies, morally completes, or finally secures humanity. The project may be Christ-measured; it is not Christ.

## Claim Integrity Grammar

Every major public or governance claim should answer:

- **What do we morally owe?**
- **What has the corpus actually designed?**
- **How can it fail?**
- **What evidence do we actually have?**
- **What evidence would force a downgrade?**

Claims that cannot answer these questions should be softened, moved to a hypothesis section, or removed.

## Missing Scenario Coverage

The fairness layer should eventually add year-scale scenarios for:

- addiction and relapse
- single parent with adolescent conflict
- rural isolation
- language access and immigrant elders
- false accusation and appeal speed
- clique-controlled local communities
- disaster displacement
- year-ten status hardening
- expensive permanent need
- honest small-business failure

These scenarios matter because fairness breaks first in ordinary mess, not in ideal cases.

## Tone Standard

Preferred language:

- "This is a serious design claim with named moral stakes, named failure modes, and a public evidence path."
- "This mechanism is designed to..."
- "This remains unproven until..."
- "A pilot should downgrade the claim if..."

Avoid:

- "solves"
- "proves"
- "guarantees" when delivery is not evidenced
- "ready" unless the relevant readiness gate has been passed
- language that treats the system as morally complete

## Annex Opening Standard

When an annex is next revised, its first screen should answer:

| Field | Question |
|---|---|
| **Plain-language purpose** | What is this annex for? |
| **Who it protects** | Whose dignity, access, freedom, or accountability is at stake? |
| **What could go wrong** | What abuse, capture, exclusion, or failure path does it guard against? |
| **Evidence status** | Is this designed, active-unproven, partly tested, or evidence-backed? |
| **Linked risks** | Which threats, patches, claims, or hardening items does it connect to? |

This standard should be applied gradually, beginning with the highest-risk annexes: identity, capacity measurement, Essential Access continuity, Voice and Service Record, demurrage, Shared Storehouse, founding legitimacy, implementation drift, and essential-sector refusal.
