# Pilot Site Selection Criteria

This document governs site selection for the first built-from-scratch pilot community established under the Humane Constitution. These criteria are binding on the Founding Board and cannot be waived by majority vote. Waiver of any disqualifying criterion requires unanimous Founding Board consent plus external civil-society review.

**Relationship to other documents:** This document is referenced in the Pilot Evidence Roadmap Phase 0 founding cascade gate. Site selection must satisfy all required characteristics before the Phase 0 gate can clear.

---

## Section 1 — Required Characteristics

The selected site must satisfy all of the following:

**1.1 Legal standing.** The site must exist within a jurisdiction where residents retain all federal constitutional protections, including Fourth and Fourteenth Amendment rights. No site in a jurisdiction that requires waiver of these protections as a condition of residency is eligible.

**1.2 Water security.** The site must have legally secured access to potable water sufficient for the projected maximum enrollment — either through senior surface water rights, a functioning municipal water system, or a demonstrated aquifer with hydrological study confirming 50-year adequacy at full enrollment.

**1.3 Agricultural viability.** The site must include or have documented access to sufficient arable land to produce a meaningful fraction of caloric needs for enrolled residents (target: 30% within 5 years of full occupancy), with soil testing confirming viability.

**1.4 Emergency service access.** The site must be within 60 minutes driving time of a hospital with trauma services (Level III or higher) and within 30 minutes of fire and emergency medical services.

**1.5 Land tenure security.** The site must be held in a legal structure that protects against involuntary sale or dissolution driven by a single actor — either a Community Land Trust, a nonprofit with mission-lock provisions, or equivalent. A simple deed to a for-profit entity does not qualify.

The land trust or equivalent structure must include: (a) a mission expressly stated as preservation of the pilot community; (b) a successor trustee board with no majority control by any single individual or entity; (c) dissolution provisions requiring supermajority vote plus a 12-month wind-down period during which enrolled residents have right of first refusal for alternative tenure arrangements; (d) bylaws published and accessible to all residents.

**1.6 External legal interface.** The site must be located within reach of county or municipal court infrastructure capable of serving as Layer 2 dispute resolution per the Jurisdiction Interface Clause. A site more than 90 minutes from a functioning courthouse is disqualified unless a formal agreement with the county court is in place before activation.

**1.7 Consent of any existing residents.** If the site has existing residents, no pilot activation may occur without documented individual consent from those residents. Consent is individual and cannot be granted by a municipal government on behalf of residents.

**1.8 Climate and disaster risk.** The site must score below the 50th percentile on a composite natural disaster risk index — combining flood, wildfire, tornado, and hurricane risk — for the selected state, using FEMA National Risk Index or equivalent. Sites in the top quartile for any single hazard are disqualified unless engineered mitigation is already in place and documented.

## Section 2 — Disqualifying Characteristics

A site is disqualified if any of the following are true:

**2.1** Located in a FEMA-designated 100-year flood plain without engineered flood mitigation already in place.

**2.2** Requires residents to waive any federal statutory or constitutional right as a condition of entry.

**2.3** Water rights are junior to senior appropriators who could legally deprive the site of water during drought conditions without remedy.

**2.4** No functioning road access capable of emergency vehicle ingress.

**2.5** Located in a jurisdiction where the pilot's governance instruments would be preempted by state law in ways that prevent the pilot from testing its core hypotheses.

**2.6** Land held by a single individual or for-profit entity with the power to terminate the pilot unilaterally.

---

## Section 3 — Preferred Characteristics (Weighted but Not Required)

**3.1** Existing distressed municipality with intact infrastructure (water, sewer, roads, some housing stock) — accelerates timeline significantly; avoids building legal personhood from scratch.

**3.2** USDA climate zone 7b–10 or demonstrated four-season food production capacity with greenhouse supplement.

**3.3** State regulatory environment permitting intentional community governance, alternative dispute resolution, and special district formation. States with high fit: Texas (minimal zoning outside incorporated cities, strong CLT law), New Mexico (strong alternative community precedent, low land cost), Tennessee (low regulatory burden, four seasons, agricultural tradition).

**3.4** Proximity to a university or research institution capable of independent audit and pilot evaluation — required for the P-019 external-validity gate and Pilot Evidence Roadmap scale-up review.

**3.5** Low composite natural disaster risk (flood, wildfire, tornado, hurricane).

**3.6** State with statutory recognition of community land trusts and arbitration agreements that include unconscionability protections for economically vulnerable parties.

---

## Section 4 — Selection Process

**4.1** The Founding Board commissions an independent site feasibility study before any site is designated. The study must include: hydrological assessment, soil and agricultural assessment, legal review of land tenure and regulatory environment, and disaster risk analysis. The commissioning party for the study may not have a financial interest in any candidate site.

**4.2** The feasibility study is published for 60 days of public comment before site ratification. Any person may submit comments.

**4.3** Existing residents of any candidate site must be individually notified and given opportunity to participate in the comment period.

**4.4** The site selection decision is published in the Founding Capital Disclosure before any resident recruitment begins.

## Section 5 — Phase 1 Minimum Viable Capital Reference

A Phase 1 pilot of 100–200 residents requires approximately $15–22M in capital:

| Category | Estimated range |
|---|---|
| Land acquisition (CLT structure, 200–400 acres rural) | $1M–$4M |
| Water system | $1M–$2.5M |
| Microgrid (solar, storage, backup) | $1.5M–$3M |
| Roads and site prep | $500K–$1.5M |
| Housing (50–80 units, modular/panelized) | $9M–$16M |
| Farm infrastructure | $600K–$1.5M |
| Clinic (primary care) | $400K–$1M |
| School (K–8, may share with commons) | $600K–$1.5M |
| Great hall and commons | $400K–$1M |
| Contingency reserve (25%) | $3.5M–$5M |

This range is achievable without a dominant single funder. See Founding Capital Framework for concentration limits and funder eligibility.

## Local Production Preference

The pilot community should target local production of essential-category goods as the primary supply model. Local production reduces ongoing external dependency and strengthens INV-005 (reality anchoring) by making physical capacity directly observable.

Target: within 5 years of full occupancy, at least 30% of caloric needs, 80% of energy needs, and 100% of water needs are met from locally managed sources.

Local production infrastructure (farms, greenhouse, microgrid, water treatment) costs 30–50% more upfront than import dependency. This premium is offset within 4–8 years through reduced ongoing external payments and substantially reduced essential-sector capture risk. See ACL-013.
