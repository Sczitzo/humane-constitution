# Elevator Pitch — Revised Direction (Plain Language)

**Status:** Proposed

> **Note on relationship to the corpus.** This pitch describes a *simplified, revised direction* that came out of stress-testing the five-instrument design. It keeps the diagnosis (money has too many jobs; survival and public power should not be for sale to the highest bidder) but proposes a different mechanism: instead of making survival goods *unbuyable* behind non-convertibility walls, it makes survival *unconditionally affordable* for everyone. It has not been adopted; it sits alongside the five-instrument design as a candidate correction to it.

---

## The pitch

> Money shouldn't decide whether you eat or whether you count in public life — but you can't fix that with walls that need guards forever. So instead of making survival *unbuyable*, we make it *unconditionally affordable*: a constitutionally indexed cash floor, funded by taxing wealth people **get without creating it** — valuable locations, natural resources, big inheritances — instead of taxing work. And money is locked out of politics at its actual choke points: campaign finance, lobbying, revolving doors. Every piece can be piloted today inside existing law. No founding moment, no measurement bureaucracy, nothing to smuggle across a wall — a floor everyone can stand on instead of walls someone has to defend.

**One-breath version:**

> Don't make survival unbuyable — make it unconditionally affordable. A cash floor indexed to the survival basket, funded by taxing what people get without creating it, with money barred from politics at its choke points. Pilotable today, no wall-keepers required.

---

## What gets taxed — in plain language

The funding principle is simple: **tax what people *get* without creating it, never what people *make*.** Wages, businesses, and inventions are untouched. Three kinds of unearned windfall carry the system instead.

### 1. Land — the location, not the building

A downtown lot in a growing city becomes valuable because of everything *around* it: roads, schools, transit, businesses, people. The owner didn't create that value — the community did.

- **Example:** Two identical houses. One sits in a small town and is worth $150,000. The other sits near a new subway stop downtown and is worth $900,000. The $750,000 difference wasn't built by the owner — it came from the city growing around the lot. That location value is what gets taxed. Anything the owner actually built or improved — the house, the renovation, the garden — is never taxed.

### 2. Resource rents — the free profit from what nature made

Oil in the ground, minerals, timber, fishing waters, even the airwaves your phone signal travels on. Nobody made these. A company that pumps oil earns something real for the *work* of finding and pumping it — but the oil itself was just *there*.

- **Example:** A company spends $20 to extract a barrel of oil and sells it for $80. The $20-plus-a-fair-profit part rewards real work and risk. The remaining windfall exists only because the company holds a license to something nature made and no one created. That windfall — the "it was just there" part — is what gets taxed, not the work.

### 3. Inherited windfalls — big money for being born to the right parents

Not the family home. Not a parent's workshop, tools, or working farm — those pass on freely. What's taxed is large fortunes passed down that the heir did nothing to earn.

- **Example:** One person inherits their mother's house and her carpentry tools and keeps working — untouched. Another inherits a $50 million portfolio at 25 and never needs to work. The second is a windfall of birth, not of effort, and it's the kind of transfer that builds permanent dynasties. Above a generous household threshold, it contributes back.

## How this differs from how things are now

| | **How things are now** | **Revised direction** |
|---|---|---|
| **Survival** | Food, housing, and medicine must be bought; lose your income and you can lose all three. Welfare exists but is conditional, means-tested, and can be withdrawn. | A cash floor, guaranteed to everyone unconditionally, indexed so it always covers the survival basket (food, shelter, care, transit). No forms, no conditions, no cutoff. |
| **What's taxed** | Mostly work: income tax on wages, payroll taxes, sales taxes on everyday purchases. Land, resource windfalls, and inheritances are lightly taxed or full of loopholes. | Mostly windfalls: location value, natural-resource profits, large inheritances. Wages, ordinary purchases, and businesses people actually build go untaxed or lightly taxed. |
| **Money and politics** | Money buys influence legally: campaign donations, lobbying, and the revolving door between regulators and the industries they regulate. | Hard structural blocks at those exact choke points — public campaign financing / democracy vouchers, lobbying limits, cooling-off rules — instead of trying to wall off all of civic life. |
| **How change happens** | Big reforms require sweeping legislation or don't happen at all. | Every piece — cash-floor pilots, land-value tax, campaign-finance rules — can be trialed today, city by city, inside existing law. No founding moment required. |
| **Enforcement** | Complex tax codes chased by armies of accountants; loopholes everywhere. | Land can't move offshore, oil can't hide, and large inheritances are visible events — the tax base is the hardest kind to dodge. A cash floor needs almost no policing at all. |

---

## Why this framing (one paragraph)

Every wall needs a wall-keeper, and wall-keepers are politics again. The five-instrument design guards survival with non-convertibility walls that must be enforced forever; this direction closes the same coercion channel from the other side — not by making survival goods unbuyable, but by making sure everyone can always buy them. A floor everyone can stand on doesn't need defending.
