# Life and Rights Under a Live System

> Losing money, status, or civic standing must not cancel your right to eat, drink, stay sheltered, travel for basics, or receive essential care.

This document describes, in plain language, what the Humane Constitution is designed to protect if it is ever adopted and made real: the rights every person would hold, how those rights are guarded against abuse, how companies operate inside the system, and what a normal year might feel like for ordinary people.

These rights and rules are **not live today**. They describe the intended design after adoption, funding, measurement, and public review. For where the project stands now and where to begin, see [Start Here](00_start_here.md).

## What you would get as a right

Under a live system, every person would have a **basic needs floor**.

That floor would include:

- food
- water
- shelter
- essential healthcare and medicines
- basic transit

The system calls this **Essential Access**.

It is not a favor. It is not a reward. It is not a benefit that can be taken away to punish you.

### What money cannot buy

The system's ordinary money is called **Flow**.

Flow can buy market goods and services. It cannot buy:

- your basic needs floor
- public power
- extra rights
- immunity from rules
- control over another person's basic access

A wealthy person may live better than you. They cannot buy the right to decide whether you eat, receive medicine, or count as a person.

### What this means in daily life

- Losing your job does not erase your basic access to essentials.
- Getting sick, disabled, displaced, or overwhelmed does not erase your personhood.
- Not joining public service does not put your survival floor at risk.
- Not having money does not make your basic rights optional.
- Having money does not let someone else buy control over your basic rights.

## Civic tools, and their limits

The system has two civic tools:

- **Voice:** helps set public priorities in limited ways.
- **Service Record:** records useful public service and helps choose people for rotating service roles.

These tools cannot become social credit.

They cannot buy:

- food
- housing
- money
- rights
- legal protection
- rank
- human worth

Participation can affect these civic tools over time. It cannot reduce your basic needs floor.

## Protections against abuse

### No coercion

No official, provider, or institution may use your Essential Access as leverage.

No one may threaten your food, shelter, medicine, or basic transit to make you obey.

### No general surveillance

The system must use the least data needed. Identity and transaction records are not for general monitoring.

### No permanent silent error

If the system makes a mistake, you must have:

- review of the decision
- continued access to the basic needs floor during review
- public correction if the mistake is confirmed

### Constitutional Integrity Panel

Some rules are treated as so fundamental that an ordinary government should not be able to quietly rewrite them. The system protects those rules with a small watchdog body that is hard to capture: an independent 7-member panel whose members serve overlapping terms (so no single government appoints them all at once) and whose budget is locked in by the constitution (so it cannot be starved out). Changing any of the most fundamental rules requires this panel's sign-off first. The governing coalition cannot appoint it, and it cannot be defunded except by the same hard-to-pass process used for those fundamental rules. The formal name is the Constitutional Integrity Panel (CIP). Its purpose is structural: to stop a future government from rewriting fundamental rights through normal legislation.

### No permanent emergency rationing

During a real shortage, the system may use **Shared Storehouse**, which means temporary rationing.

It must be public, time-limited, and reviewable. It cannot become the normal system.

### If something goes wrong

| What happened | What the system must do |
|---|---|
| Your Essential Access was blocked | Review quickly and restore access during the dispute. |
| A rule was applied to you by mistake | Protect basic accounts and give you a review path. |
| Emergency rationing was started | Publish the reason, time limit, and end process. |
| Measurement data is disputed | Default toward keeping basic access while the dispute is reviewed. |

When a dispute is about whether someone is who they say they are, or whether they qualify, the system is deliberately tilted toward keeping people in rather than shutting them out. The reasoning is that wrongly cutting off a real person from food or medicine is far worse than wrongly keeping a borderline case covered, so the rules are set to make the first kind of mistake the rare one (the formal name is the Asymmetric Error Doctrine). To keep that honest, the system publishes, every quarter, how often it is wrongly excluding vulnerable people, with hard caps on that rate. And any exclusion that drags on more than 14 days without a formal, documented decision is automatically reversed.

Silence about a serious failure is itself a failure.

## How firms operate here

> The system does not ban profit. It bans using survival needs as a hostage.

Large companies may make money, grow, compete, and invest. What they may not do is gain power by threatening people's access to food, water, shelter, medicine, energy, or basic transport.

### Profit is still allowed

Big companies are not automatically treated as enemies.

A company may:

- earn profit
- pay workers
- raise capital
- build factories
- sell useful goods
- compete for customers
- improve technology
- expand if it follows the rules

The point is not to make every company small or public. The point is to stop any company from becoming powerful enough to say, in effect, "Do what we want or people lose the basics they need to live."

### What is not allowed

The system draws a hard line against hostage power.

A company may not use control over essentials to force the public, workers, patients, or governments into surrendering basic rights.

That means a company cannot lawfully say:

- "Pay whatever we demand or people lose medicine."
- "Drop the investigation or fuel stops moving."
- "Give us special immunity or food supply breaks."
- "Let us ignore safety rules or hospitals go without supplies."
- "Punish our critics or we will cut off essential service."

Disputes over price, contracts, taxes, wages, permits, and rules can still happen. But those disputes must not be fought by putting survival access at risk.

### Energy, medicine, and other essential producers

Oil, energy, medicine, logistics, water, housing, and food companies can still earn Flow when they produce real, verified value. For example:

- an energy company can earn Flow for verified fuel, electricity, storage, or grid support
- a medicine company can earn Flow for verified safe medicines and reliable supply
- a logistics company can earn Flow for verified delivery of essential goods
- a food producer can earn Flow for verified production and distribution

The key word is **verified**. The system rewards actual production, quality, delivery, and reliability. It does not reward artificial scarcity, hidden sabotage, or threats to withhold essentials for leverage.

### A profitable compliant path

The system is meant to give companies a clear and profitable way to comply. A company that produces essentials well should not have to choose between obeying the rules and surviving as a business: if it makes useful things, proves delivery, treats essential access as protected, and follows safety and anti-coercion rules, it can be paid.

That matters because a system that only punishes companies invites evasion. A better system makes the lawful path the practical one:

- produce what people need
- document what was produced
- get paid for verified delivery
- keep ordinary profit from non-essential markets
- resolve disputes through review instead of coercion

The goal is not revenge against business. It is to make honest production more profitable than holding the public hostage.

### Refusal and retaliation cannot break survival access

A company may refuse a bad contract. It may challenge a rule. It may sue, appeal, lobby, bargain, or leave a non-essential market.

What it may not do is retaliate by cutting off survival access.

If an essential producer refuses to cooperate or tries to punish the public, the system must protect the basic needs floor first. That can include emergency purchasing, substitute suppliers, temporary public operation, rationing during real shortage, or other lawful continuity measures.

Before a company is allowed to become the only supplier of something essential, the system lines up backups in advance: other suppliers are signed up ahead of time, with contracts already agreed and ready to go. Those standby contracts switch on by themselves — no new government decision needed — if the main supplier stops delivering for 14 days straight, walks away from the market, or grows large enough to trip a pre-set warning line on how much of the market one company controls (a "Watch-level" concentration threshold). The point is that a supplier threatening to walk away gains no leverage, because the replacement is already contracted before the threat is ever made.

The important rule is simple:

> A fight with a company must not become a fight over whether people eat, receive medicine, stay warm, or keep basic transport.

### Foreign and domestic chokepoints

Foreign companies can trade if they follow the essential-access rules that apply to the goods or services they provide.

If a foreign country refuses those rules, or retaliates by threatening essentials, trade does not become a free-for-all. It moves through **Annex AT**, the system's trade pathway for non-compliant foreign countries. In plain terms, Annex AT means:

- the country can still be dealt with where necessary
- essential access remains protected
- trade terms must account for coercion risk
- the public is not required to accept hostage pressure as normal commerce

This is especially important for energy, medicine, food, rare materials, shipping, and other goods where sudden disruption could harm ordinary people.

The same protection applies domestically. A company inside the system's borders that controls a critical chokepoint — whether energy, food, medicine, logistics, or data networks — must follow the same rules. It cannot use that control to force concessions or hold the survival floor hostage, no matter whether the company is foreign or domestic.

If a company with that kind of leverage refuses to cooperate with the Essential Access system and people's survival is at genuine risk, the government can step in and temporarily run that specific function itself — fuel delivery, medicine distribution, power grids, or whatever is at stake — while a permanent replacement supplier or competitive solution is found (the formal name is a temporary public receiver authority, the same idea as a court appointing a caretaker to keep a vital service running). This is designed to be rare, short-term, and narrowly focused on the function itself. It is an emergency brake, not a tool for permanent nationalization or punishment.

### What this means in practice

The same principle reads three ways depending on who you are:

> **To a compliant company:** Make useful things, prove what you delivered, follow the rules, and you can profit.

> **To a non-compliant company:** You may argue, appeal, and compete, but you may not use people's survival needs as leverage.

> **To the public:** Big companies can exist and profit here. No company gets to turn basic survival into a bargaining chip.

## A year in the system: ordinary lives

These stories are not promises. They are pressure tests.

They ask what a year might feel like for ordinary people if the system existed: where it protects dignity, where it still creates friction, and where fairness becomes hard. The point is realism. Not utopia. Not disaster fantasy. A normal year with work, boredom, resentment, care, ambition, mistakes, and limits.

### Talia: the unstable ordinary worker

Talia works warehouse shifts and food-service weekends. She is not political. Most weeks she works, sees friends, streams shows, helps her nephew, and ignores public meetings.

In February her hours are cut. In April her mother has surgery, so Talia misses shifts and loses a supervisor's favor. In June she changes jobs. In August her rent share rises after a roommate leaves. By November she is working steadily again, but she has no cushion and is tired.

The system should keep job instability from becoming hunger, untreated illness, or survival panic. It should not make Talia spend her worst months proving hardship through forms she barely has time to complete.

**Fairness test:** Can the system protect normal instability without turning hardship into an application process?

### Devon: work, play, repeat

Devon works a steady maintenance job, plays basketball twice a week, follows sports, takes one modest trip, and uses Voice only when an issue directly affects him.

Most people may live closer to Devon than to the highly engaged civic ideal. They work, rest, care for family, spend Flow, complain occasionally, and mostly let public machinery run in the background.

That is not failure. A system that requires constant civic attention from ordinary people is already too fragile.

**Fairness test:** Does the system work when most people are neither heroic nor hostile, just occupied with daily life?

### Lena: the caregiver artist

Lena paints, sells a few pieces, and cares for her brother after a stroke. Her contribution is real but irregular, intimate, and hard to verify.

In March her brother's condition worsens. In May she misses a community exhibition. In July she applies for recognition of caregiving contribution but finds the process intrusive. In October she sells two paintings and feels both proud and financially behind.

The system should honor care without turning household life into a monitored workplace. It should let art matter without pretending every artist will earn enough Flow to live comfortably.

**Fairness test:** Can the system honor unpaid care and creative work without creating fake metrics for private life?

### Aaron: the person who mostly exists

Aaron does not work, volunteer, campaign, make art seriously, or care for dependents. He spends time online, walks around town, eats simply, socializes occasionally, and avoids responsibility.

Nothing dramatic happens. He receives Essential Access. He has little Flow. He cannot afford many comforts. Friends drift away because they are busy. In December he is still alive, materially basic, and socially thin.

This is the hardest moral test because Aaron is not secretly productive. If survival is truly unconditional, he remains covered. But equal survival does not mean equal lifestyle, authority, admiration, Flow, or choice.

**Fairness test:** Is the system willing to protect bare human existence while preserving reward and relief for those carrying real burdens?

### Nia: the high-burden skilled worker

Nia is an emergency nurse. Her work is skilled, stressful, and socially necessary. She believes in the dignity floor, but she is exhausted by the number of people depending on systems she helps keep running.

Winter illness strains the hospital. Summer shortages create longer shifts. In October she considers leaving for less stressful work. She sees non-workers secure in basic needs and feels conflicted: she does not want them harmed, but she wants the burden recognized.

The system must not morally blackmail skilled workers into endless sacrifice. It must reward hard work without turning skill into rule over others.

**Fairness test:** How does the system retain excellence without letting excellence become dominance?

### Omar: the small builder

Omar repairs appliances and wants to expand into a cooperative repair shop. He is ambitious, practical, and not trying to dominate anyone.

In February he leases a small space. In April he hires an apprentice. In June cash flow is tight. In August he holds a larger balance for equipment purchases; the small-operator reserve means ordinary working balances sit below the net-worth demurrage floor. In November the shop becomes viable.

The system should make room for ambition, risk, tools, inventory, and working capital. It should distinguish useful building from disguised hoarding.

**Fairness test:** Can someone become materially better off by building useful capacity without converting wealth into power over others?

### Nolan: the rule-gamer

Nolan is clever and opportunistic. He tests boundaries: trading favors for Essential Access items, overstating hardship, creating fake contribution records, and routing Flow through friends.

In March he finds a side market. In June he recruits others. In August a provider notices patterns. In October enforcement begins. Nolan says he is being persecuted and tries to turn the process into a political dispute.

The system should catch organized abuse without treating every poor, confused, disabled, traumatized, or desperate person as suspicious.

**Fairness test:** Are enforcement tools aimed at scalable harm rather than merely visible low-level misuse?

### Mara and Eli: the dissenting household

Mara and Eli dislike the system's moral and political assumptions. They use Essential Access because refusing it would hurt their children, but they do not participate in Voice and are considering leaving.

In May their children still receive school meals and healthcare through Essential Access. In September the family explores exit. In December they remain because grandparents live nearby and relocation is expensive.

The system should protect dissenters without requiring gratitude. It should not use children, access, or local reputation as pressure points.

**Fairness test:** Can someone reject the system and still receive dignity, due process, and practical freedom?

### Caleb: addiction and relapse

Caleb is 31. He cycles through recovery, relapse, shame, and renewed attempts to stabilize. He misses appointments, loses his wallet twice, and sometimes uses support badly. He is not easy to help. He is also not disposable.

In February he starts treatment. In May he relapses and misses a review deadline. In July a provider flags suspicious redemption patterns. In September he is clean again but afraid the system now sees him only as a risk.

The system should keep his basic access alive while still protecting providers, neighbors, and public trust. It must distinguish illness, manipulation, desperation, and organized abuse.

**Fairness test:** Can the system respond to relapse without either abandoning the person or pretending harm never happened?

### Ruth: rural isolation

Ruth is 68 and lives in a rural county with thin transit, weak broadband, one clinic, and few approved vendors. On paper, she has Essential Access. In practice, getting to the food pickup, pharmacy, or appeal office takes time she does not always have.

In March the clinic loses two staff members. In June wildfire smoke shuts down travel for several days. In October a digital notice tells her to confirm information online, but her connection fails.

The system should not count access as real merely because an account exists. Rights must reach people through distance, weather, weak infrastructure, and low provider density.

**Fairness test:** Does the right still mean anything when delivery is inconvenient, rural, offline, and expensive?

### Anika: false accusation

Anika is a single parent with two teenagers. A neighbor reports her for selling Essential Access items after seeing several household exchanges. The accusation is wrong: she was coordinating groceries with another parent during a school closure.

In April her account is flagged. In May a local official tells her the review will take weeks. In June the accusation is cleared, but she has already missed work, lost trust, and felt publicly shamed.

The system needs abuse detection. It also needs fast restoration, privacy, and public correction when accusations are false.

**Fairness test:** Can the system investigate abuse without making accusation itself a punishment?

### Mateo: language access

Mateo is 74. He immigrated decades ago, speaks conversational English, and reads official notices poorly. His daughter usually helps, but she moves away for work.

In January he misunderstands a notice about provider changes. In April he misses an appeal window. In July he attends a civic meeting but cannot follow the terms. By November he has stopped trying to participate.

The system should not require policy fluency, perfect English, or digital confidence for a person to keep access and voice.

**Fairness test:** Are rights real for people who cannot understand the system's language, forms, deadlines, or meetings?

### Elise: disaster displacement

Elise loses her apartment in a flood. Her documents, medications, and phone are gone. She crosses into a neighboring jurisdiction to stay with relatives.

In the first week she needs medicine, food, transit, and identity recovery. In the second month she still does not know whether she is temporary, relocated, or starting over.

The system should preserve continuity across disaster, lost documents, and jurisdiction boundaries. A person should not become administratively invisible at the exact moment they are most vulnerable.

**Fairness test:** Can the system follow a person through displacement faster than paperwork can fail them?

### Dr. Sen: the expensive permanent need

Dr. Sen has a rare condition that requires expensive medicine every month. The need is permanent, unpopular in budget meetings, and hard for ordinary people to understand.

In March supply tightens. In June public frustration rises because a small group of patients requires costly support. In September a proposal appears to narrow eligibility through technical language.

The system's dignity floor is tested most sharply when a person's need is expensive, permanent, and not broadly relatable.

**Fairness test:** Does the system protect unpopular need, or only cheap and emotionally sympathetic need?

### Omar again: honest failure

Omar's repair shop could also fail. A supplier backs out, the apprentice leaves, and his equipment purchase was mistimed. By December the shop closes.

He tried to build useful capacity and failed honestly. He should not be treated as a rule-gamer, parasite, or moral failure. He also should not be shielded from every consequence.

The system should let people take real risks without turning failure into destitution or fraud suspicion.

**Fairness test:** Can the system preserve enterprise when honest failure is common?

### Year Ten: status hardening

Ten years later, informal classes may form even if the rules forbid formal rank. People with strong Service Records may become insiders. Low-Flow people may be quietly dismissed. Skilled professionals may form guild-like influence. Frequent Voice participants may dominate meetings because everyone else is tired.

No constitution can prevent all human status games. But it can watch for them, name them, and refuse to let informal prestige become control over survival or public power.

**Fairness test:** Does the system detect status hardening after years of ordinary habit, not only during founding?

### What these stories reveal

The central fairness tension is not worker versus non-worker. It is burden versus unconditional dignity.

A humane system must protect Aaron, who mostly exists, without exploiting Nia, who carries emergency work, or erasing Lena, whose care is real but hard to measure.

That means four things must stay distinct:

| Layer | Fairness rule |
|---|---|
| Survival | Protected because the person is human. |
| Comfort and choice | Unequal through Flow, work, risk, creativity, and trade. |
| Public power | Not purchasable through wealth, popularity, or organized pressure. |
| Trust and responsibility | Earned for role eligibility, never used as human worth. |

## How to check these rights

The full rule text is in the [Humane Constitution](../constitution/Humane_Constitution.md).

The [Threat Register](../governance/Threat_Register.md) lists known ways these rights could be attacked or weakened.

In a live system, a rights challenge would go through review, appeal, and Ombuds paths. In this repository, a reader can open an issue naming the rule, the failure, and the evidence.
