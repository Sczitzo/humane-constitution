# How Big Companies Work Here

> The system does not ban profit. It bans using survival needs as a hostage.

This is a plain-language explanation of how large companies would operate under the Humane Constitution if the system were ever adopted and made real.

These rules are **not live today**. They describe the intended design: companies may make money, grow, compete, and invest, but they may not gain power by threatening people's access to food, water, shelter, medicine, energy, or basic transport.

## Profit Is Still Allowed

Big companies are not automatically treated as enemies.

A company may:

- earn profit
- pay workers
- raise capital
- build factories
- sell useful goods
- compete for customers
- improve technology
- expand if it follows the rules

The point is not to make every company small or public. The point is to stop any company from becoming powerful enough to say, in effect, "Do what we want or people lose the basics they need to live."

## What Is Not Allowed

The system draws a hard line against hostage power.

A company may not use control over essentials to force the public, workers, patients, or governments into surrendering basic rights.

That means a company cannot lawfully say:

- "Pay whatever we demand or people lose medicine."
- "Drop the investigation or fuel stops moving."
- "Give us special immunity or food supply breaks."
- "Let us ignore safety rules or hospitals go without supplies."
- "Punish our critics or we will cut off essential service."

Disputes over price, contracts, taxes, wages, permits, and rules can still happen. But those disputes must not be fought by putting survival access at risk.

## Energy, Medicine, And Other Essential Producers

Oil, energy, medicine, logistics, water, housing, and food companies can still earn Flow when they produce real, verified value.

For example:

- an energy company can earn Flow for verified fuel, electricity, storage, or grid support
- a medicine company can earn Flow for verified safe medicines and reliable supply
- a logistics company can earn Flow for verified delivery of essential goods
- a food producer can earn Flow for verified production and distribution

The key word is **verified**. The system rewards actual production, quality, delivery, and reliability. It does not reward artificial scarcity, hidden sabotage, or threats to withhold essentials for leverage.

## A Profitable Compliant Path

The system is meant to give companies a clear and profitable way to comply.

A company that produces essentials well should not have to choose between obeying the rules and surviving as a business. If it makes useful things, proves delivery, treats essential access as protected, and follows safety and anti-coercion rules, it can be paid.

That matters because a system that only punishes companies invites evasion. A better system makes the lawful path practical:

- produce what people need
- document what was produced
- get paid for verified delivery
- keep ordinary profit from non-essential markets
- resolve disputes through review instead of coercion

The goal is not revenge against business. The goal is to make honest production more profitable than holding the public hostage.

## Refusal And Retaliation Cannot Break Survival Access

A company may refuse a bad contract. It may challenge a rule. It may sue, appeal, lobby, bargain, or leave a non-essential market.

What it may not do is retaliate by cutting off survival access.

If an essential producer refuses to cooperate or tries to punish the public, the system must protect the basic needs floor first. That can include emergency purchasing, substitute suppliers, temporary public operation, rationing during real shortage, or other lawful continuity measures.

The important rule is simple:

> A fight with a company must not become a fight over whether people eat, receive medicine, stay warm, or keep basic transport.

## What About Foreign Companies And Countries?

Foreign companies can trade if they follow the essential-access rules that apply to the goods or services they provide.

If a foreign country refuses those rules, or retaliates by threatening essentials, trade does not become a free-for-all. It moves through **Annex AT**, the system's trade pathway for non-compliant foreign countries.

In plain terms, Annex AT means:

- the country can still be dealt with where necessary
- essential access remains protected
- trade terms must account for coercion risk
- the public is not required to accept hostage pressure as normal commerce

This is especially important for energy, medicine, food, rare materials, shipping, and other goods where sudden disruption could harm ordinary people.

## What This Means In Practice

For a compliant company, the message is:

> Make useful things, prove what you delivered, follow the rules, and you can profit.

For a non-compliant company, the message is:

> You may argue, appeal, and compete, but you may not use people's survival needs as leverage.

For the public, the promise is:

> Big companies can exist here. Big companies can profit here. But no company gets to turn basic survival into a bargaining chip.
