# Three-Minute Explainer

## Core script

Most systems make one mistake over and over again: they force too much through the same rail, money. The same money that decides whether you can eat, stay housed, get care, or travel for basics also helps decide who can invest, who gets institutional influence, and who can shape public life. That is how wealth turns into coercion.

The Humane Constitution tries to stop that conversion by design.

Its core move is simple: separate the functions that most systems collapse together.

It uses **Flow** for markets: wages, prices, contracts, saving, enterprise, and investment.

It uses **Essential Access** for the survival floor: food, water, shelter, essential healthcare and medicines, and basic transit.

It uses **Voice** for bounded civic priority-setting.

It uses **Service Record** for readiness to serve in rotating public roles.

And it uses **Shared Storehouse** only during genuine verified shortage, when scarcity has to be managed openly instead of by panic prices.

The point is not the labels alone. The point is the walls between them.

**Flow** is not allowed to buy survival access or formal civic rule.

**Essential Access** is not cash. It cannot be hoarded into status or converted into ordinary market money.

**Voice** and **Service Record** are not social credit. They cannot buy goods, rights, immunity, or dignity. They are bounded civic instruments, not a score of human worth.

**Shared Storehouse** is not supposed to become permanent. It is a temporary emergency tool for real shortage.

So the design is trying to make three things true at once.

First, no one should lose the basics because they lost income.

Second, markets should still work. People should still be able to build, trade, save, create, and take initiative.

Third, wealth should not quietly purchase civic control.

That does not mean the design is finished or ready for instant rollout. It is honest about that. The repo already includes the constitutional text, the public white paper, the threat register, the patch log, and adversarial simulations. But it still needs pilot evidence, a proven non-coercive identity stack, and a legitimate founding coalition before anyone should pretend it is scale-ready.

So the right response to this project is not blind faith and not casual dismissal. The right response is serious critique, hard testing, and specific questions.

Because the central claim is worth testing: survival, markets, and governance should not all ride on the same rail.

## Delivery notes

- Read it steadily, not triumphantly.
- Put the five instruments on screen as they are named.
- Pause slightly after `The point is the walls between them.`
- The last sentence should land as the thesis, not as a slogan.

## Short closing variant

`The whole project stands or falls on one question: can survival, markets, and governance be separated strongly enough to stop wealth from becoming coercion?`
