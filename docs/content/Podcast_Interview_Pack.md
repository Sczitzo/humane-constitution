# Podcast Interview Pack

These questions and answers are designed for long-form interviews. The answers stay source-grounded and should be treated as model responses, not mandatory wording.

## 1. What is the Humane Constitution in plain English?

**Model answer:** It is a constitutional design built around one core separation: survival, markets, and governance should not all ride on the same money. The system uses Flow for markets, Essential Access for the survival floor, Voice for bounded civic priority-setting, Service Record for readiness to serve in rotating public roles, and Shared Storehouse for real emergency shortage.

## 2. What problem are you actually trying to solve?

**Model answer:** The core diagnosis is that wealth turns into coercion when the same money decides who survives, who builds, and who governs. Most systems fail at that interface. The project tries to stop that conversion by design instead of merely condemning it morally.

## 3. Are you anti-market?

**Model answer:** No. Flow still runs prices, wages, contracts, saving, enterprise, and investment. The project is not anti-market. It is anti-conversion of market money into survival leverage and civic rule.

## 4. Why not just reform the existing system instead of designing a whole new constitutional architecture?

**Model answer:** Incremental reform can matter, but the project starts from the view that some failures are structural, not cosmetic. If the same rail still governs survival, market power, and political access, then reforms can soften outcomes without changing the underlying conversion problem.

## 5. Why five instruments? Why not keep it simpler?

**Model answer:** Because one instrument doing every job creates one concentrated lane of power. The system is trying to assign different social functions to different instruments so that losing one kind of leverage does not automatically destroy every other form of security or public standing.

## 6. Is Essential Access just welfare with better branding?

**Model answer:** No. The design treats baseline survival as a constitutional floor rather than a discretionary program. That distinction matters because the whole point is to stop survival from being used as political leverage.

## 7. Is Voice or Service Record basically a social credit score?

**Model answer:** No. The constitution explicitly rejects any measure of human worth. Voice and Service Record cannot buy goods, rights, immunity, or survival access. They are bounded civic instruments, not a general ranking system.

## 8. What makes you think this would resist corruption?

**Model answer:** It may not fully resist corruption. The stronger claim is narrower: the system is designed to make corruption harder, more visible, and more challengeable. That is why the repo includes a threat register, patch log, explicit invariants, oracle hardening, and review triggers.

## 9. What is Shared Storehouse, and why not just let prices manage shortage?

**Model answer:** Shared Storehouse is a temporary emergency rationing instrument used during verified shortage. The design rejects the idea that panic prices are a fair way to decide who goes without essentials during crisis.

## 10. What is the hardest unresolved problem?

**Model answer:** Identity without coercion or surveillance is one of the hardest unresolved problems. The repo is explicit that the project still needs a proven non-coercive identity stack and real pilot evidence before scale claims would be credible.

## 11. Does this depend on people being morally good?

**Model answer:** No. The design assumes greed, capture, fear, and manipulation will happen. That is why it relies on structural separation, public challenge paths, review institutions, and adversarial modeling rather than trust alone.

## 12. What would ordinary life feel like in this system?

**Model answer:** In plain terms, losing a job would not automatically mean losing food, shelter, medicines, or basic transit. Markets would still exist. Businesses would still be built. But the ability to turn wealth into civic control would be more tightly constrained.

## 13. Why keep saying the system is not scale-ready yet?

**Model answer:** Because overstating readiness would weaken the whole project. The honest position is that the design is much stronger as a constitutional and systems document than as an immediate rollout plan. It still needs pilots, identity proof, and founding legitimacy.

## 14. What kind of criticism do you most want?

**Model answer:** Specific criticism. Attack the boundary assumptions. Attack the measurement layer. Attack the anti-capture mechanisms. Attack the identity model. The useful response is not vague disbelief but concrete pressure on whether the separations can actually hold under stress.

## 15. What is the strongest one-line summary?

**Model answer:** Survival should not depend on income, and wealth should not be able to buy civic rule.

## Host notes

- Push the guest on identity, complexity, corruption, and rollout.
- Avoid softballs that only invite slogans.
- The most interesting interviews will test whether the separation of functions really survives contact with institutions, scarcity, and bad actors.
