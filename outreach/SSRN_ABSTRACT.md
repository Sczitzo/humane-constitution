## Abstract (~280 words)

Most social systems fail at a single interface: when survival, enterprise, and political influence all ride on the same monetary instrument, wealth converts into coercion. Welfare systems become leverage. Markets price the desperate. Governance is purchased. This paper presents the Twelve-Pillar Protocol — a constitutional architecture designed to structurally separate these three social functions rather than legally promise their separation.

The protocol introduces four non-convertible instruments: LC (a non-monetary survival entitlement delivering a defined basket of essentials), EC (an enterprise currency governing markets and wages), DW/CR (a bounded civic influence layer), and SQ (emergency rationing during verified physical shortage). The architectural claim is that the walls between these instruments are the system. Where most welfare proposals add programs within an existing monetary architecture, this protocol replaces the monetary architecture itself.

The design is developed using adversarial red-teaming methodology. A versioned threat register documents 25 attack vectors — each scored by impact, likelihood, and detectability — with linked mitigation patches and explicit residual risk statements. Twenty-one patches address threats ranging from above-ledger bypass attacks (convertibility that circumvents instrument separation without touching formal ledgers) to oracle epistemological capture (measurement systems that satisfy formal independence requirements while sharing underlying theoretical frameworks) to democratic backsliding under electoral pressure. A formal acceptance protocol governs how patches move from design to operation, including a bootstrap instrument resolving the circular dependency in governance system self-activation.

The protocol is honest about its incompleteness: founding coalition decisions are explicitly marked as open commitments requiring pilot evidence; 9 pre-launch blocking gates must be cleared before operation. The full design is open-source (CC BY 4.0) and actively soliciting adversarial critique.
